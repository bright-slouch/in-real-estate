# Transaction Deadline Checklist — Central Indiana

> Reference for `re-transaction-manager`. Complete guide to every transaction deadline: what it means, who owns it, what happens if missed, and how to request an extension.

---

| Deadline | Typical Day | Calendar or Business? | Owner | Auto-Waiver if Missed? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Earnest money deposit | Days 1–3 | Business days | Buyer | No — constitutes potential default |
| Inspection period expiration | Day 10 | Calendar days | Buyer | **Yes — contingency auto-waives** |

## Earnest Money Deposit

**Standard:** 2–3 business days from acceptance; check purchase agreement for exact language.

**Held by:** Buyer's brokerage, listing brokerage, or title company — as specified in the PA.

**What happens if missed:**
- Buyer is in potential default of the contract
- Listing agent must notify seller immediately
- Seller may have the right to terminate with notice to buyer
- Do NOT silently accept late earnest money without a written amendment confirming late delivery

**How to handle late earnest money:**
1. Contact buyer's agent immediately
2. Confirm when earnest money will be delivered
3. Execute a written amendment confirming the revised delivery date (IAR Form 157 or addendum)
4. Confirm receipt with the holding entity in writing

**Interpleader note:** If the deal falls through and parties dispute earnest money, Indiana courts allow title companies and brokerages to file interpleader — a legal process to let a court decide the disposition. This takes months. Encourage mutual release first.

## Inspection Period

**Standard:** 10 calendar days from acceptance. Some competitive-market offers use 7 days.

**Calendar days:** Day 10 means by the end of Day 10, even if it falls on Sunday or a holiday. There is no grace period.

**What the buyer can do during inspection:**
- Order home inspection (general inspector + radon test)
- Order specialty inspections: sewer scope, WDI (termite), pool, HVAC, chimney
- Review inspection reports and decide on a response

**Buyer's options before the deadline:**
1. Submit IAR Form 162 — request repairs or credit (does NOT waive the contingency until resolved)
2. Submit IAR Form 162 — accept the property as-is (releases the contingency)
3. Submit IAR Form 162 — terminate the contract and request earnest money refund

**What happens if NOTHING is submitted by Day 10:**
The inspection contingency **automatically waives**. The buyer has accepted the property in its current condition. This is one of the most consequential auto-waivers in Indiana residential practice.

**Agent must calendar Day 10 with a 48-hour warning.** If the buyer hasn't scheduled an inspection by Day 3, escalate.

**Requesting an inspection extension:**
- Use IAR Form 157 (Amendment) to extend the inspection period
- Must be agreed to by seller before the original deadline expires
- Typical reason: specialist not available (radon remediation specialist, structural engineer, sewer scope company)
- Draft language: "Inspection period is hereby extended from [original date] to [new date]. All other terms remain unchanged."

## Inspection Response (IAR Form 162)

**Form:** IAR Form 162 — Inspection Contingency Response

**Submitted by:** Buyer's agent to listing agent

**Standard deadline:** Within the inspection period (Day 10), or per contract language specifying a response window

**Seller's response (IAR Form 163):**
- Standard: within 2–3 business days of buyer's response
- Options: accept, counter, or reject
- If seller rejects all requests without counter and buyer is still within inspection period: buyer may terminate

**Escalation:** If inspection response negotiation runs close to the inspection deadline, both parties should agree in writing (via amendment) to extend the period while negotiating.

## Appraisal Contingency

**Standard:** 21 calendar days from acceptance; can range 18–25 days depending on market.

**What it protects:** Buyer's right to renegotiate or terminate if the appraised value is below the purchase price.

**What happens at the deadline:**
- If appraisal has come in at value: contingency releases automatically or by notice
- If appraisal is low: buyer and seller are in appraisal gap negotiation
- If buyer does nothing by the deadline: **contingency auto-waives** — buyer is committed to purchase at contracted price regardless of appraisal

**Critical agent action:** Confirm appraisal value with lender before the contingency deadline. If value isn't in yet, request a written extension from seller (IAR Form 157).

**If appraisal hasn't been completed by Day 18:**
Alert agent — appraisal is behind schedule. Contact lender for status. If appraisal won't be complete before Day 21, request extension immediately.

**Extension language:**
"Appraisal contingency deadline is hereby extended from [original date] to [new date] to allow time for appraisal completion and review. All other terms unchanged."

---

## Financing Contingency

**Standard:** 21 calendar days from acceptance. Gives lender time to process underwriting.

**What it protects:** Buyer's right to terminate and receive earnest money return if financing is not approved.

**Auto-waiver risk:** If the financing contingency expires without a termination notice, the buyer may lose their earnest money claim if they later fail to close due to financing.

**Lender communication:** Buyer's agent should confirm with lender at Day 18 whether financing is on track. If not: request extension immediately.

**When a financing extension is needed:**
- Draft: "Financing contingency deadline is hereby extended from [original date] to [new date]. Buyer's lender requires additional time for [underwriting / appraisal review / condition clearance]."
- Seller has the right to refuse. If seller refuses and financing isn't secured by the original deadline, buyer's position is at risk.

**After the financing contingency passes:**
Buyer is committed. If financing later falls through, seller may have a claim to the earnest money as liquidated damages. Agent must consult managing broker immediately.

## Title Commitment

**Issued by:** Title company, typically Day 20–28.

**Contents:** Preliminary title report showing:
- Current vesting (who owns it, in what form)
- Exceptions (easements, restrictions) that will appear on the policy
- Requirements (liens, mortgages, judgments that must be cleared before closing)

**Common Indiana title issues:**
- Unreleased prior mortgages (refinancing where old mortgage wasn't properly released)
- Outstanding HOA special assessment liens
- IRS or Indiana DOR tax liens
- Estate probate not complete
- Boundary disputes on older platted neighborhoods
- Mechanic's liens from recent improvements

**If a title issue is discovered:**
1. Contact both parties and the title underwriter immediately
2. Determine if the issue can be resolved before closing
3. If title resolution takes time: draft closing date extension
4. Title company underwriters sometimes insure over minor exceptions — not available for all issues

**Agent action:** Never close on a property with an unresolved title issue that could affect the buyer's ownership or the lender's security interest.

---

## Loan Commitment / Clear to Close

**Issued by:** Lender, typically Day 28–35.

**Clear to Close (CTC):** Lender has approved the loan and is ready to fund. Does NOT mean closing is fully scheduled — closing must still be coordinated with title.

**Before CTC, lender may need:**
- Updated pay stubs or bank statements (within 30 days of closing)
- Explanation letters for large deposits
- HOA certification for condo purchases
- Homeowners insurance binder (confirmed by lender)
- Gift letter if part of down payment is a gift
- Verification of employment (VOE) — sometimes done the day before closing

**If CTC is not issued by Day 30:**
Agent should contact lender directly to identify the specific condition holding up CTC. Help buyer respond to document requests same day.

**Closing Disclosure (CD):**
- Lender must deliver CD to buyer **at least 3 business days before closing** (federal TRID requirement)
- Closing cannot proceed if CD was not delivered on time
- If closing must be moved, CD delivery resets — lender must deliver a revised CD with the new 3-day window

## Final Walkthrough

**Timing:** Typically 24–48 hours before closing; some agents do the morning of closing.

**Purpose:**
- Verify property is in substantially the same condition as at time of offer
- Confirm all agreed repairs are completed (if seller made repairs instead of credit)
- Confirm all included personal property remains
- Confirm property is clean and vacant (or has an occupancy agreement in place)

**What to inspect:**
- All appliances included in sale (test them)
- All faucets, toilets (run briefly)
- HVAC (test seasonal operation)
- All light fixtures and outlets
- Garage door openers functional
- All keys and access codes present
- No new damage from moving or maintenance

**Issues found at walkthrough:**

| Severity | Response |
|---|---|
| Minor (e.g., one appliance not cleaned) | Note and request resolution before keys transfer |
| Moderate (e.g., agreed repair not done) | Closing holdback: seller funds held in escrow |
| Major (e.g., flood damage, missing appliances) | Delay closing; notify all parties; consult broker |

**Do NOT close** if major undisclosed damage is discovered at walkthrough. This is one of the most common errors in Indiana residential closings.

## Closing Day

**Location:** Title company office; buyer and seller may close simultaneously or at separate appointments.

**Closing sequence:**
1. Buyer and seller review and sign closing documents (deed, mortgage, settlement statement, all addenda)
2. Buyer provides certified funds (per CD amount)
3. Lender funds the loan (wire transfer to title company)
4. Title company records the deed with county recorder's office (same day, usually within hours)
5. After recording confirmed: keys are transferred

**Funding gap:** In Indiana, closing and funding typically happen same day. Buyer does not take possession until the deed records.

**Post-closing agent tasks:**
- Confirm with title that deed has recorded
- Remove lockbox and for-sale sign (if listing side)
- Transfer keys and garage openers to buyer
- Update ShowingTime (set property to sold/off market)
- Send congratulations communication (see `re-closing-coordinator`)
- Log closing in transaction management system

## Extension and Amendment Quick Reference

| Situation | Form | Who Initiates | Key Content |
|---|---|---|---|
| Extend inspection period | IAR Form 157 | Buyer's agent | New inspection deadline date |
| Extend appraisal contingency | IAR Form 157 | Buyer's agent | New appraisal deadline date; reason |
| Extend financing contingency | IAR Form 157 | Buyer's agent | New financing deadline; lender status |
| Extend closing date | IAR Form 157 | Either party | New closing date; reason |
| Post-possession agreement | IAR Form 196 | Listing agent | Daily rent, security deposit, move-out deadline |
| Price amendment | IAR Form 157 | Either party | New price; reason (appraisal gap, inspection credit) |
| Repair credit at closing | IAR Form 157 | Either party | Credit amount; what items it covers |
| Terminate with EM return | IAR Form 157 or mutual release | Buyer's agent | Earnest money disposition |

## Earnest Money Disposition Quick Reference

| Scenario | Who Gets Earnest Money | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Buyer terminates within inspection contingency | Buyer | Contingency provides release |
| Buyer terminates within financing contingency | Buyer | Contingency provides release |
| Buyer terminates within appraisal contingency | Buyer | Contingency provides release |
| Seller defaults (refuses to close, title defect seller refuses to fix) | Buyer | Plus potential damages claim |
| Buyer defaults after all contingencies waived | Seller | As liquidated damages |
| Mutual agreement to terminate | Split or per agreement | Use mutual release form |
| Disputed disposition | Interpleader / court | Last resort; takes months |
