## Inspection Negotiation — Detailed Tactics

### Cost Reference Guide for Common Indiana Inspection Findings

Use these ranges when buyer lacks contractor estimates. Always recommend obtaining actual bids for accuracy.

**HVAC:**
- Gas furnace replacement: $3,500–$7,000 (standard efficiency) / $5,000–$10,000 (high efficiency)
- Central AC replacement: $3,500–$6,500
- Furnace + AC combo: $7,000–$14,000
- HVAC service/tune-up only: $150–$300
- Ductwork repairs: $500–$3,000 depending on scope

**Roofing:**
- Asphalt shingle replacement (2,000 sqft home): $8,000–$18,000 depending on pitch and complexity
- Roof repair (minor): $500–$2,000
- Soffit/fascia repair: $500–$2,500
- Gutter replacement: $1,000–$3,000

**Electrical:**
- Panel upgrade (100A → 200A): $2,500–$5,000
- GFCI outlet installation: $150–$300 per location
- Knob-and-tube remediation: $8,000–$20,000+ depending on scope
- Aluminum wiring pigtailing (ARC-FAULT): $50–$150 per outlet

**Plumbing:**
- Polybutylene (PB) pipe replacement: $4,000–$20,000+ depending on home size
- Sewer line repair (root intrusion): $3,000–$12,000 / full replacement: $8,000–$25,000
- Water heater replacement: $900–$1,500 (gas) / $1,200–$2,500 (electric)
- Minor plumbing repairs: $200–$800

**Foundation:**
- Crack repair (cosmetic, hairline): $300–$1,000
- Crack repair (structural, active): $3,000–$20,000+ depending on method
- Basement waterproofing (interior): $5,000–$15,000
- Carbon fiber straps (bowing wall): $3,000–$8,000 per wall section

**Radon:**
- Mitigation system installation: $800–$1,200 typical in Central Indiana
- Test: $150 additional (often inspector includes for $100–$150 add-on)

**Mold:**
- Small area remediation (< 10 sqft): $500–$1,500
- Moderate area (10–100 sqft): $2,000–$6,000
- Large-scale/structural mold: $10,000–$30,000+; may require scope expansion

**Environmental:**
- Asbestos testing: $250–$500
- Asbestos abatement: $1,500–$30,000 depending on location and scope
- Lead paint testing: $250–$400 (XRF analysis)
- Lead paint abatement (component removal): varies widely

## Inspection Response Negotiation Tactics

### Tiered Ask Strategy

When buyer has multiple issues, present a tiered request:

**Tier 1 — Non-negotiable:**
Items that represent genuine safety hazards or major financial exposure. Buyer will terminate without resolution.
*Example: failing furnace in winter, active roof leak, failed sewer scope*

**Tier 2 — Important:**
Significant issues where the buyer wants some coverage but has flexibility on amount.
*Example: roof with 3–5 years remaining, aging HVAC, electrical panel concerns*

**Tier 3 — Nice-to-have:**
Minor items included to establish negotiating room. Buyer would drop these to close a Tier 1/2 gap.
*Example: interior cosmetic issues, minor plumbing, deferred landscaping*

**The ask:** Lead with the total, then be willing to remove Tier 3 items if seller counters reasonably.

### Framing an Inspection Credit Request

**Template:**
> "Following the inspection completed by [Inspector Name] on [Date], buyers request a $[Total] closing cost credit to address the following findings: [List Tier 1 and Tier 2 items with approximate costs]. All other inspection findings are accepted as-is and buyers waive further inspection-related contingencies upon credit agreement."

**Key elements:**
- Dollar amount (not a repair list) — sellers prefer credits for speed and certainty
- Specific items listed (not all 47 inspector observations)
- Release language — gives seller clarity that the credit ends the inspection negotiation
- Inspector name and date — establishes documented basis for the request

### Seller Response Tactics

**Accept:**
Use when the credit is small relative to home value and closing the deal is the priority.

**Counter:**
Most common response. Counter at a number that addresses Tier 1 items. Example:
> "Seller will provide a $[X] closing cost credit for the [specific items]. Seller accepts all other inspection findings. Buyer agrees to waive all remaining inspection-related contingencies upon execution of this addendum."

**Reject and Explain:**
Only when price was already adjusted for known condition. Example:
> "The property was priced to reflect its condition and the market analysis factored in anticipated deferred maintenance. The seller is not in a position to provide additional concessions. Buyer may proceed on current terms or terminate per the inspection contingency."

**Offer to Repair Instead of Credit:**
Occasionally the right approach but risky:
- Specify who does the work and what quality standards apply
- Set a repair completion deadline (before closing)
- Require buyer's agent approval of completed repairs before closing
- Do NOT recommend this for major systems (buyers rarely trust seller-initiated work)

## Appraisal Gap Strategies — Detailed

### When the Appraisal Comes in Low

**Immediate steps for buyer's agent:**
1. Get the appraisal report from the lender (buyer or agent can request a copy)
2. Review the comps the appraiser used — are they the right comps?
3. Check: did the appraiser miss any recent, nearby, similar closed sales?
4. Note the effective date of the appraisal and any market condition adjustments

**ROV (Reconsideration of Value) — Is It Worth Pursuing?**

Worth pursuing if:
- Agent can identify 2+ closed comparables within 6 months/1 mile that the appraiser didn't use
- Those comps support a higher value by at least 50% of the gap amount
- Timeline allows for ROV without blowing the closing date (add 5–10 business days)

Not worth pursuing if:
- Appraiser used correct comps and the value is defensible
- Gap is < $5,000 (buyer and seller are likely better off splitting it)
- ROV delay would cause the deal to fall apart

**Splitting the Gap — The Most Common Resolution:**

Formula for splitting:
- Total gap: $15,000
- Seller reduces price: $7,500 (to $492,500 from $500,000)
- Buyer brings extra cash: $7,500
- Both parties give up something; both want the deal

Agent framing for seller:
> "I know this feels unfair — you got the price you wanted from a willing buyer. But the appraiser looked at recent sales and the market said $485,000. We have two choices: find another buyer who either pays cash or has gap coverage, or work with this motivated buyer who wants to close. If we re-list, the next appraisal will likely come back in the same range. Here's what I'd recommend: split the gap. You get $492,500 instead of $500,000. You still net more than the next-best offer we've seen."

### Lender Constraints on Appraisal Gap

Conventional loans: Lender will only loan against appraised value (not purchase price)
- If purchase price = $500K, appraisal = $485K, lender loans against $485K
- Buyer must bring the $15K gap as cash IN ADDITION to their down payment
- Buyer needs to confirm with their lender they have this cash available

FHA loans: Same constraint — no financing above appraised value; gap must be cash
VA loans: Tidewater Initiative — appraisal comes in low → VA gives agent opportunity to provide comps before appraisal is finalized; if still low, buyer may exercise their option to terminate without forfeiting earnest money

## Market-Condition Negotiation Adjustments

### Deep Seller's Market (< 1.5 months supply)

**Buyer tactics:**
- Expect to offer asking price or above
- Concession requests after inspection should be surgical — major safety items only
- Avoid inspection waivers — use shortened periods (7 days) instead
- Emotional state management is critical — buyers lose bids; help them stay rational

**Seller tactics:**
- Counter conservatively or not at all — market supports seller
- If inspection credit requested, smaller counter is defensible: "Market conditions support a minimal concession given current demand"
- Multiple backup offer leverage: "We have backup offers. Our counter expires in 4 hours."

### Balanced Market (3–6 months supply)

**Buyer tactics:**
- Inspect fully; request a reasonable credit for major items
- Don't be aggressive on minor items (it can kill deals over small money)
- Appraisal gap: split strategy often works well in balanced market

**Seller tactics:**
- Be responsive to reasonable inspection requests — losing a buyer in balanced market means starting over with less momentum
- Counter at 60–70% of what buyer asked as a starting point

### Buyer's Market (> 6 months supply)

**Buyer tactics:**
- Full inspection, full credit request; seller has limited leverage
- Consider requesting warranty as part of inspection resolution
- Appraisal gap: seller bears more of the gap; buyer has walk-away power

**Seller tactics:**
- Most important thing: keep the buyer engaged at all costs
- Accept reasonable inspection credits rather than risk termination and re-listing
- Gap concessions are often necessary — market won't support the price; renegotiate

## Multiple Offer Management (Listing Agent)

### Setting Highest and Best — Script

**To all buyers' agents (send via email with timestamp):**
> "We have received [X] offers on [Property Address]. The seller is requesting highest and best offers be submitted by [Date] at [Time]. Please resubmit your client's offer with their highest and best terms by that deadline. We will present all offers to the seller at that time and respond to each party by [Date + 1 business day]."

**What NOT to say:**
- Don't reveal competing offer prices ("you're currently at $X, highest offer is $Y")
- Don't hint at what terms the seller favors beyond the stated criteria
- Don't extend the deadline multiple times — it creates the impression of a fake process

### Presenting Multiple Offers to Seller — Framework

**Step 1:** Provide a written comparison (re-offer-writer generates this)

**Step 2:** Walk through each offer's strengths and risks:
- Highest price offer with weak financing: "This is the highest number. But FHA on a property with [condition issue] creates real risk. If the FHA appraiser flags [issue], we're renegotiating anyway. Net risk-adjusted value: [X]."
- Lower cash offer: "This offer is $[X] less but eliminates all appraisal and financing risk. Close in 15 days. Certainty has value."

**Step 3:** Give the seller a clear recommendation but respect their final decision:
- "My recommendation is Offer [X] because [2–3 data-backed reasons]."
- "That said, this is your home and your decision. I want to make sure you understand every option."

**Step 4:** Get signed acceptance or counter — never leave a verbal agreement

### Counter to Multiple Buyers — Legal Safety

As noted in the offer-terms guide: if countering multiple buyers simultaneously, the counter MUST include language that:
1. It is being sent to multiple buyers
2. First executed counter returned to listing agent constitutes acceptance
3. All other counters become void upon acceptance of one

Failure to include this language risks creating two simultaneous binding contracts.

## Emotional Coaching — Full Scripts

### Buyer Who Has Lost 3+ Bids

> "[Name], I want to take a few minutes away from the tactical details and just talk about where we are. You've been at this for [X months]. You've made [Y offers]. That's real effort, and I know it's exhausting.

> Here's what I see: You're not losing because of bad luck. You're in [market name] in a market where [data: absorption rate, DOM, competition level]. The buyers who are winning are [doing X differently].

> I want to give you two options. Option 1: we adjust [price ceiling / search area / contingency approach] and I think our success rate changes significantly. Option 2: we take a 2-week break and reset. Sometimes the right home hasn't come on the market yet, and I'd rather us be fresh when it does.

> What would help you most right now?"

### Seller Getting Anxious About DOM

> "[Name], I hear you — it feels like the house should have sold by now. Let me give you the actual picture.

> In [area], the average days on market right now is [X days]. You're at [Y days]. [If Y < X: 'You're actually ahead of pace.'] [If Y > X: 'You're past average, which tells us something.']

> Here's what the data is saying: [activity data — showings, feedback, offers or lack of]. Based on this, I think [one of the following: the price needs to move / the marketing needs adjustment / we need to be patient / we have a strategic timing issue to address].

> I'm not going to tell you everything is fine if it isn't. My honest assessment: [honest assessment]. Here's what I recommend we do: [specific action]. Let's give it [X days] and reassess."

### Dealing with an Adversarial Agent

**Principles:**
1. Never match their tone in writing — you look unprofessional and create a paper trail
2. State your client's position clearly and factually
3. If they are misrepresenting a contract term, cite the contract section directly
4. If they make threats, acknowledge them without escalating: "I've noted your position. I'll communicate it to my client and respond in writing by [time]."
5. Report genuine ethics violations to MIBOR after the transaction is complete — not during, unless it rises to fraud

**Script for handling a demanding agent:**
> "I understand you're frustrated. My client's position is [position], and it's based on [specific contract term / market data / inspection finding]. I'm happy to work through this constructively. If there's a specific concern about the contract terms, let's address those directly. What specific resolution would work for your client?"

## Walk-Away Decision Matrix

Before advising a client to terminate, run through this checklist:

| Question | Yes Points Toward | No Points Toward |
|---|---|---|
| Is the issue documented in writing? | Proceeding safely | Uncertainty — get it documented first |
| Is the gap more than 2% of purchase price? | Walk if unresolved | May be worth absorbing |
| Has seller made any good-faith counter? | Continue negotiating | Walk |
| Does client have emotionally healthy relationship with property? | Continue negotiating | Walk (decision may already be made) |
| Are there other suitable properties currently available? | Walk with confidence | Continue negotiating |
| Does client's lender support proceeding under current terms? | Continue | Walk or renegotiate |
| Is the issue resolvable post-close (client takes on the cost)? | Continue | Walk |

**The honest conversation with a client before walking:**
> "Let me be straight with you: you have a legal right to terminate using this contingency. Your earnest money would come back. But before you make that call, let's make sure you've considered: [what would closing anyway cost you? What's the alternative? Is this deal worth saving at a different number?] I'll support whatever decision you make. I just want you to make it with full information."
