# Example Prompts: re-neighborhood-research

Six scenarios demonstrating how to invoke the skill with different buyer profiles and research needs.

---

## Scenario 1: Chicago Relocation Family — Schools Focus

**Buyer Profile:** Married couple with two kids (ages 7 and 10). Relocating from Naperville, IL for one spouse's new position at Eli Lilly's corporate campus. Budget $550,000-$650,000. Schools are the non-negotiable priority — they are used to highly-rated suburban schools and want that same caliber in Indiana.

**Prompt:**
```
/re-neighborhood-research — Carmel 46032, family of 4 relocating from Naperville IL, husband starts at Eli Lilly in 6 weeks. Budget $600K. Schools are top priority — they're used to Naperville 203 schools. Also need commute time to Lilly's campus at 893 Delaware. Kids are 7 and 10.
```

**What this report should lead with:**
- Carmel Clay Schools profile — high school, middle, elementary assignment for 46032 (West Clay elementary area)
- GreatSchools directional context with required caveat
- Side note: confirm whether specific address falls in Carmel Clay vs. HSE (boundary verification instruction)
- Commute from Carmel 46032 to 893 Delaware St via US-31 S to I-465 E: ~25-35 min depending on time (morning peak on US-31 is known congestion)
- Hamilton County tax estimate on $600K home: ~$5,000-$7,000/year with homestead
- Monon Trail access, Clay Terrace shopping, IU Health North proximity
- New construction vs. resale context in that zip

**Key agent positioning:** "Carmel Clay Schools rank among the top school systems in Indiana and are consistently mentioned in national publications. Let me show you exactly which elementary school feeds to which middle and high school based on the specific streets you'd be looking at."

## Scenario 2: Young Professional, First Home — Broad Ripple / Walkability Focus

**Buyer Profile:** Single woman, 29, software developer working remotely 3 days/week; goes to Salesforce Tower (201 S Meridian, downtown) 2 days/week. Budget $280,000-$325,000. Wants walkable neighborhood, restaurant/bar scene, access to running trails. Not focused on schools. Dog-friendly neighborhood matters.

**Prompt:**
```
/re-neighborhood-research — Broad Ripple Village area, Marion County, first-time buyer. Works remote + 2 days downtown at Salesforce Tower. Budget $300K. Priorities: walkability, Monon Trail access, restaurant/bar scene, dog friendly. Not worried about schools.
```

**What this report should lead with:**
- Walkability context: Broad Ripple has among the highest walk scores in Indianapolis; Monon Trail runs directly through the neighborhood
- Monon Trail: 28 miles, connects Broad Ripple north to Hamilton County (Carmel, Westfield) and south to downtown; well-maintained; key selling point for active buyers
- Commute to Salesforce Tower (downtown): via College Ave or Meridian, ~15-20 min by car; IndyGo Red Line bus option (Broad Ripple to downtown) noted
- Marion County (MSD Washington Township) tax context for ~$300K home: $4,500-$6,000/year depending on township
- Flood zone note: properties near the White River and Broad Ripple Ave have historically had some flood risk — check FEMA for specific address
- Broad Ripple Village dining/nightlife (Goose the Market, Bosphorus, The Vanguard, etc.)
- Property type context: mix of older SFR (bungalows), condos, and some newer townhomes in this price range

**Key agent positioning:** "Broad Ripple is one of the few truly walkable urban neighborhoods in Indianapolis. The Monon Trail access alone makes it stand out. For your budget and lifestyle, this is where you get the most character and convenience."

## Scenario 3: Comparison Report — Westfield vs. Noblesville

**Buyer Profile:** Couple, two kids in elementary school. Budget $420,000-$470,000. Moving within the metro (currently renting in Carmel). Open to either Westfield or Noblesville — want to understand the tradeoffs. Both spouses commute to different parts of town: one downtown, one to I-69/96th St tech corridor.

**Prompt:**
```
/re-neighborhood-research — Compare Westfield vs Noblesville for a family, budget $440K, two kids elementary age. One parent commutes downtown, other to Fishers tech corridor near I-69/96th. Want to understand school differences, commute reality, what you get for the money in each.
```

**What this report should lead with:**
- Side-by-side structure: two columns or clearly labeled sections for each city
- **Schools:** Westfield Washington Schools vs. Noblesville Schools — both Tier 2; Westfield slightly newer facilities due to growth-era investment; both solid
- **Commute (downtown):** Westfield via US-31/I-465: 35-45 min; Noblesville via SR-37/I-69/I-465: 35-50 min (SR-37 construction ongoing); both comparable, Westfield slightly more direct
- **Commute (Fishers I-69 corridor):** Noblesville has an advantage — direct SR-37 (becoming I-69) south into Fishers; Westfield requires going east before south
- **What $440K buys:** In Westfield, likely newer construction (post-2010) in a subdivision; Grand Park area. In Noblesville, could be newer construction OR older established neighborhood near historic downtown square; more variety
- **Tax comparison:** Both Hamilton County; similar effective rates; Noblesville slightly lower per some districts
- **Character:** Westfield = sports/active families (Grand Park); Noblesville = historic downtown feel, Ruoff Music Center entertainment

**Key agent positioning:** "Both are excellent Hamilton County communities at your budget. The real deciding factor is commute — for the Fishers tech corridor, Noblesville has a clear advantage on SR-37. If downtown is the bigger commute priority, they're roughly equal."

## Scenario 4: Rural Property, Hendricks County — Well/Septic, Commute Reality Check

**Buyer Profile:** Couple, no kids, mid-30s. Want land — at least 2 acres, maybe a horse or chickens. Budget $375,000. Looking at rural Hendricks County between Brownsburg and Danville. One works from home; other commutes to Indianapolis International Airport (southwest side). First-time rural buyers.

**Prompt:**
```
/re-neighborhood-research — Rural Hendricks County, somewhere between Brownsburg and Danville. Looking for 2+ acres, okay with older home, possibly want chickens or small animals. Budget $375K. One works from home, other commutes to Indy airport area. First-time rural buyers — need to understand well/septic.
```

**What this report should lead with:**
- **Well and septic explainer** (lead here — critical for first-time rural buyers):
  - No water/sewer bill (advantage); maintenance costs are buyer's responsibility (reality check)
  - Well: recommend water quality test before closing; test for bacteria, nitrates, hardness, and any locally-relevant contaminants
  - Septic: strongly recommend septic inspection as part of due diligence; Indiana does not require seller to disclose septic system age in all cases; a failed septic system can cost $8,000-$25,000+ to replace
  - Note: Hendricks County Health Department can pull records on permitted septic systems
- **Commute to Indianapolis International Airport (via I-74 W to I-465 S):** Brownsburg to airport ~25-35 min; Danville to airport ~30-40 min. I-74 corridor is generally better than north corridors — less congestion
- **Zoning and animals:** Unincorporated Hendricks County allows agricultural use on most parcels; verify with Hendricks County Planning for specific parcel zoning before making offer
- **Tax context:** Hendricks County rates ~1.2-1.7%; rural parcels often have agricultural soil deductions if any acreage is farmed — ask county auditor
- **School district:** Depending on location, could be Brownsburg, Danville Community, or North Hendricks — verify by address
- **No HOA:** Rural parcels typically have no HOA; verify there are no deed restrictions recorded with Hendricks County Recorder

**Key agent positioning:** "Rural property is a different buying experience than suburban. I want to make sure you go in with eyes open on well and septic — let's make sure those inspections are built into the offer so you're not surprised after closing."

## Scenario 5: Downsizing Couple — Zionsville HOA Communities

**Buyer Profile:** Couple, early 60s. Kids grown. Selling their Carmel home after 22 years. Want to stay in the area but downsize to a maintenance-free home — villa or paired patio home preferred. Budget $550,000-$700,000. Care about community amenities, walkability to Zionsville's brick Main Street, and keeping taxes manageable.

**Prompt:**
```
/re-neighborhood-research — Zionsville area, downsizing couple, kids grown, want maintenance-free villa or patio home. Budget $600K. Want to be walkable to Main Street, care about HOA amenities (pool, fitness, social events), want lower taxes than what they're paying in Carmel Clay. No school focus.
```

**What this report should lead with:**
- **Zionsville character context:** Brick Main Street, mature trees, boutique shopping and dining; one of the most charming downtown corridors in the Indianapolis suburbs; walkability premium
- **HOA community overview:** Several villa/patio-home communities exist within walking/biking distance of Main Street; general characteristics and what to look for in HOA documents for this buyer type
- **HOA document checklist tailored to downsizers:**
  - Maintenance-free definition: what's covered (lawn, snow, exterior) vs. what's not (HVAC, roof in some communities)
  - Age restrictions: some communities have 55+ designations — confirm if that matches or doesn't match the buyer
  - Social amenities: pool, fitness center, clubhouse
  - Rental restrictions: important if buyer ever wants to lease
  - Reserve fund: aging villa communities need adequate reserves for shared infrastructure
- **Tax advantage:** Zionsville sits partly in Boone County (lower tax rates: 1.1-1.5%) vs. the Hamilton County portions; a $600K Zionsville home may have a lower tax bill than their current Carmel Clay home
- **Commute irrelevant for this buyer** — but note: proximity to US-421 and I-65 if family visits from elsewhere

**Key agent positioning:** "Zionsville gives you the walkable small-town lifestyle without giving up the quality you're used to in Carmel. And depending on exactly where you land, your tax bill could actually go down. That's a story worth telling."

## Scenario 6: Investor — Rental Demand in Greenwood, Johnson County

**Buyer Profile:** Local investor with two existing rentals in Marion County. Looking to add a third investment property. Considering Greenwood (Johnson County) for better school-district demand, lower taxes, and tenant quality. Budget $280,000-$320,000. Wants a single-family home to hold as a long-term rental.

**Prompt:**
```
/re-neighborhood-research — Greenwood Johnson County, investor looking at single-family rental, budget $300K. Want to understand rental demand, tenant pool, school district situation (does it matter for renters?), HOA rental restrictions to avoid, and tax comparison to Marion County. Long-term hold strategy.
```

**What this report should lead with:**
- **Rental demand context:** Greenwood has a strong tenant pool — healthcare workers from the south-side hospital corridor, logistics workers from the I-65 corridor, families who want Johnson County schools but can't buy yet
- **School district for investors:** Center Grove vs. Greenwood Community Schools — Center Grove commands higher rents; Greenwood Community is strong for the price point. Identify which district the specific address falls in.
- **HOA rental restrictions (critical for investor):** Many Central Indiana HOAs restrict rentals (some limit to 20% of homes as rentals; others prohibit outright). Flag: run the CC&R check before making an offer on any HOA property for investment use
- **Tax comparison:** Johnson County (1.5-2.0% effective) vs. Marion County (2.5-3.2%); on a $300K investment property without homestead exemption, Johnson County saves $1,000-$2,000/year in taxes (note: investment properties do NOT qualify for homestead deduction — tax estimates are higher than owner-occupant scenarios)
- **No-HOA targeting:** For investors, targeting non-HOA or investor-friendly HOA communities in Greenwood increases flexibility
- **Cap rate context:** At $300K purchase, current gross rents in Greenwood SFR market run approximately $1,600-$2,000/month for 3BR/2BA depending on condition and school district. Provide rough cap rate framework.
- **I-65 proximity:** Note the south-side logistics employment corridor as a tenant demand driver

**Key agent positioning:** "For your investment criteria, Greenwood makes a lot of sense. The tenant pool is stable, taxes are materially lower than Marion County on an investment property, and you're looking at a market with real demand from renters who want the Johnson County school experience without the ability to buy yet."
