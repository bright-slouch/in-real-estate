# Indiana Disclosure Requirements Reference

> **Legal Disclaimer:** This document is an educational summary for licensed Indiana real estate agents. It does not constitute legal advice. Consult your managing broker and/or a licensed Indiana real estate attorney for guidance on specific situations.

---

## Governing Law

- **IC 32-21-5-10** — Indiana Seller's Residential Real Estate Sales Disclosure
- **IC 32-21-5-1 through 32-21-5-14** — Full disclosure chapter
- **42 U.S.C. § 4852d** — Federal Lead Paint Disclosure requirement (pre-1978 homes)
- **IAR Form 46234** — Indiana Seller's Residential Real Estate Sales Disclosure Form (current version)
- **IAR Form 46232** — Lead Paint Disclosure and Acknowledgment

---

## The Indiana Seller's Disclosure Form (Form 46234)

### Purpose

The Seller's Disclosure form informs buyers of the condition of the property as known to the seller at the time of the sale. It is not a warranty and does not guarantee condition. It is a disclosure of seller's **knowledge** of the property.

### When Required

Form 46234 is required for most residential real estate transfers, including:
- Single-family homes
- Condominiums and townhomes
- Multi-unit properties (2-4 units where seller occupies one)
- Vacant lots if represented as residential

### When NOT Required (Exemptions)

Under IC 32-21-5-3, disclosure is not required for:

1. **Court-ordered transfers** (foreclosure sale, bankruptcy, probate)
2. **Transfers between co-owners** (adding/removing a spouse, etc.)
3. **Government-owned property** transfers
4. **New construction** — builder provides new construction disclosures instead
5. **"As-is" sales** — NOTE: Sellers must STILL disclose known defects even in "as-is" sales; "as-is" only means buyer accepts the condition as disclosed, not that seller can conceal

## What Must Be Disclosed

The seller must disclose all **known material defects** that:
- Affect the value of the property, OR
- Would affect a reasonable buyer's decision to purchase

### Major Disclosure Categories (Form 46234 Sections)

**Section 1: Ownership and Use**
- How long seller has owned the property
- How the property has been used (personal residence, rental, vacant)
- Any judgments, liens, or encumbrances affecting title

**Section 2: Systems and Conditions**
The seller must disclose known problems with:
- Foundation, basement, and crawlspace (water intrusion, cracks, settling)
- Roof (age, leaks, repairs, condition)
- Electrical system (age of panel, known issues, work done without permits)
- Plumbing (galvanized pipes, septic system, well)
- HVAC (age of furnace/AC, known issues)
- Water heater (age, condition)
- Appliances included in the sale (known defects only)

**Section 3: Environmental Hazards**
- Asbestos (presence known to seller)
- Lead paint (addressed separately — see federal requirements)
- Underground storage tanks
- Radon (if tested — must disclose results)
- Methamphetamine lab cleanup (if applicable)
- Pest infestations (past or present known infestations, treatment history)

**Section 4: Water**
- Well water — age, condition, water test results if available
- Flooding history — any known flooding or water intrusion (basement, yard)
- Flood zone designation (if known, but buyer should verify independently)
- Drainage issues

**Section 5: Structural**
- Additions or modifications — were permits pulled? Is work professionally done?
- Drainage or grading issues
- Soil problems

**Section 6: Legal**
- HOA existence, dues, special assessments
- Deed restrictions or easements affecting use
- Pending legal actions involving the property
- Boundary disputes

**Section 7: Neighborhood Conditions**
- Airports or flight paths (if known to affect property)
- Commercial or industrial uses nearby (if known)

## What Must NOT Be Disclosed

Federal Fair Housing Act prohibits disclosure of:

1. **Occupant's protected class status** — cannot disclose that occupants are (or were) a particular race, religion, national origin, sex, disability status, or familial status
2. **Stigmatized property** — Indiana does not require disclosure of:
   - Deaths in the home (natural, suicide, or homicide) — IC 32-21-5-11
   - Sex offender presence in the neighborhood (per Megan's Law, buyers must check the state registry independently)
   - HIV/AIDS status of former occupants
3. **Proximity to sex offenders** — agents are prohibited from volunteering this; buyers must check VINE/Indiana Sex Offender Registry

**If buyer asks directly:** Do not provide information on stigmatized property. Say: "I'm not able to answer that question — Indiana law prohibits me from disclosing that type of information. You're welcome to research the property's history through public records."

## "As-Is" Sales

An "as-is" designation in the purchase agreement means:
- **Buyer accepts the property in its current condition**
- **Buyer typically waives or limits their inspection contingency**
- **Seller is NOT exempt from disclosing known material defects**

A seller cannot use "as-is" to conceal a known leaking roof, failing foundation, or other defect. Failing to disclose known material defects in an "as-is" sale can result in fraud claims and license revocation.

**Practical advice:** If seller wants "as-is," still require a complete, honest disclosure form. "As-is" limits the buyer's remedies after closing, but does not limit the seller's obligation to disclose.

## Lead Paint Disclosure (Pre-1978 Homes)

### Federal Requirement (42 U.S.C. § 4852d)

For any residential property built **before 1978**, federal law requires:

1. **Seller must disclose** any known lead-based paint or lead-based paint hazards
2. **Seller must provide** the buyer with any available records or reports about lead paint
3. **Agent must ensure** buyer receives the EPA pamphlet: "Protect Your Family from Lead in Your Home"
4. **Buyer must have** a 10-day window to conduct a lead paint inspection (buyer may waive this in writing)
5. Both buyer and seller must sign **IAR Form 46232** (Lead Paint Disclosure and Acknowledgment)

### Indiana Practice

- Use IAR Form 46232 alongside Form 46234 for all pre-1978 homes
- Agent must retain signed form in transaction file for at least 3 years
- Violation of federal lead paint disclosure law: civil penalty up to $10,000 per violation

### Script for Sellers of Pre-1978 Homes
> "Because your home was built before 1978, federal law requires us to complete a lead paint disclosure form. I'll need you to tell me what you know about lead paint in the home — if you've had it tested, if there's any known lead paint, or if you're not aware of any. We'll also need to provide the buyer with an EPA lead paint pamphlet and give them 10 days to do their own inspection if they want one."

## Common Disclosure Mistakes

### Errors to Avoid

1. **"I didn't know" as a defense** — If a reasonable person in the seller's position would have known about a defect, "I didn't know" may not protect against claims. Sellers who have owned a property for years should know about recurring basement flooding, even if they haven't "tested" for it.

2. **Incomplete disclosures** — Leaving sections blank is not the same as disclosing "no known issues." Walk sellers through each section.

3. **Disclosure submitted too late** — Indiana practice requires disclosure be provided to the buyer before or simultaneously with the purchase agreement. The buyer has a right to rescind if not provided timely.

4. **Forgetting to update** — If a new material fact arises after the original disclosure (e.g., roof leak discovered during listing), the seller must update the disclosure form before closing.

5. **"As-is" does not mean no disclosure** — A common seller misconception. Reinforce this at listing appointment.

6. **Agent completing the form** — The agent should not complete the disclosure form on behalf of the seller. The seller completes it; the agent's role is to explain what is required, not to fill it in.

| When | Action |
|---|---|
| Listing appointment | Provide Form 46234 to seller; walk through each section |
| Before or upon accepting offer | Buyer must receive signed disclosure form |
| Buyer's review period | Buyer has right to rescind (per IC 32-21-5-10) |
| Updated disclosure needed | When seller learns of new material fact after listing |
| Pre-1978 homes | Form 46232 required; 10-day lead paint inspection window |

## Rescission Rights (Buyer's Rights)

Under IC 32-21-5-10(b), if the disclosure form is delivered after the purchase agreement is signed:
- The buyer has **2 business days** to revoke their offer after receiving the disclosure
- The buyer must revoke in writing
- The earnest money must be returned if the buyer revokes within this window

If disclosure is delivered before or simultaneously with the purchase agreement, no additional rescission right applies beyond standard contingency rights.
