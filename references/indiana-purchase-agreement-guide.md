# Indiana Purchase Agreement Guide

> **Legal Disclaimer:** This guide is an educational summary for licensed Indiana real estate agents. It does not constitute legal advice. Consult your managing broker and/or a licensed Indiana real estate attorney for guidance on specific contract situations.

---

## Overview

The Indiana Purchase Agreement (IAR Form 150) is the primary contract for residential real estate sales in Indiana. It is a bilateral, binding contract once signed by all parties. This guide walks through each major section and explains Indiana-specific norms.

---

## Section 1: Parties

**Buyer(s):** Full legal name(s) exactly as they will appear on the deed. If buying as an entity (LLC, trust), use the full legal entity name and confirm signature authority.

**Seller(s):** Full legal name(s) exactly as they appear on current title. For estates, trusts, or LLCs, confirm the authorized signatory before submitting an offer.

**Agent tip:** If buyers are married, both names typically go on the deed and purchase agreement. If buyers are unmarried co-purchasers, both sign. If one person is buying alone despite being married, a spousal waiver may be needed — check with title company.

---

## Section 2: Property Description

Must include the complete street address, city, county, and state. Ideally include the full legal description from the county records, but at minimum the property address must be unambiguous. Include the property's parcel number (from county assessor) for precision.

**Inclusions:** List any personal property that is specifically agreed to be included (appliances, window treatments, outdoor structures that could be disputed). Indiana practice is that items physically attached to the home (built-in appliances, ceiling fans, light fixtures) are included unless specifically excluded.

**Exclusions:** List any fixtures seller wants to remove (heirloom chandelier, security cameras, specific appliances). Be specific.

---

## Section 3: Purchase Price

The agreed purchase price. State the full amount in both numbers and words. If the offer is above list price, the purchase agreement should reflect the actual offered price — not the list price.

**Escalation clauses:** Not standard in IAR forms but can be added via addendum. Define: starting price, increment, ceiling, and what documentation from competing offer is required to trigger escalation.

---

## Section 4: Earnest Money

### Indiana Norms

- **Typical range:** 1-3% of purchase price
- **Minimum reasonable:** $1,000 (on lower-priced properties)
- **Market context:** In a competitive market, higher earnest money signals buyer commitment. On a $350,000 home, $5,000-$7,000 is common; $10,500 (3%) is strong.
- **Due:** Typically within 2-3 business days of acceptance

### Earnest Money Handling

- Held in escrow by the buyer's agent's brokerage, the listing broker's brokerage, or the title company — whichever is specified in the agreement
- Not released until: closing (applied to purchase), mutual agreement of the parties, or court order in the event of a dispute
- In Indiana, if buyer defaults without legal cause, seller may keep earnest money as liquidated damages (subject to agreement terms)
- If seller defaults, buyer is entitled to earnest money refund plus may seek specific performance or damages

### Earnest Money Disputes

If the deal falls apart and parties can't agree on earnest money disposition, the holding brokerage may interplead the funds with a court. This process can take months. A written mutual release signed by both parties is the fastest path to fund release.

---

## Section 5: Financing Contingency

### Standard Indiana Practice

Most purchase agreements include a financing contingency protecting the buyer if they cannot secure a loan. Key terms:

- **Loan type:** Conventional, FHA, VA, USDA — specify
- **Loan amount:** Dollar amount (not just percentage)
- **Interest rate ceiling:** Maximum acceptable rate (typically set at current market rate + 1%)
- **Commitment deadline:** The date by which the buyer must have a written loan commitment from their lender. Standard is 25-30 days from acceptance.
- **Close date:** Must allow sufficient time after commitment for underwriting to clear

**Removal:** If financing cannot be secured by the commitment deadline, buyer can withdraw and receive earnest money back. If buyer fails to apply in good faith or fails to notify seller of inability to obtain financing, they risk losing earnest money.

**Cash purchases:** No financing contingency. Must specify "cash" and often attach proof of funds.

---

## Section 6: Home Inspection Contingency

### Standard Indiana Practice

- **Inspection period:** 10 calendar days from acceptance is the Indiana standard (can be negotiated shorter in competitive markets; 7 days is common in fast markets)
- **What it covers:** All physical aspects of the home including general home inspection, radon testing, wood-destroying insect (WDI) inspection, sewer scope, pool, HVAC system
- **Deadline:** Buyer must submit an Inspection Response (IAR Form 162) by the deadline or contingency is waived by default

### Buyer's Options After Inspection

1. **Accept as-is** — Move forward; contingency removed
2. **Request repairs or credits** — Submit IAR Form 162 with specific requests
3. **Withdraw** — If property condition is unacceptable; buyer receives earnest money refund

### Seller's Options in Response to Inspection Request

1. **Accept** — Agree to all requested repairs/credits
2. **Counter** — Offer alternative repairs or partial credit
3. **Reject** — Decline all requests; buyer must then accept, renegotiate, or withdraw

**Agent tip for sellers:** A lump sum credit at closing (reflected in the final settlement statement) is often cleaner than requiring sellers to make repairs. It avoids disputes about quality of work and simplifies the closing process.

---

## Section 7: Appraisal Contingency

### Standard Indiana Practice

Protects buyer if the home appraises for less than the purchase price.

**If home appraises below contract price, buyer's options:**
1. **Renegotiate price** — Seller lowers price to appraised value (most common resolution)
2. **Buyer pays the gap** — Buyer pays difference between appraisal and contract price out of pocket (lender only loans against appraised value)
3. **Buyer walks** — If appraisal contingency is intact, buyer can withdraw and recover earnest money
4. **Challenge the appraisal** — Agent or buyer can submit a Reconsideration of Value (ROV) with additional comparable data

**Waiving the appraisal contingency** is increasingly common in competitive markets and for cash buyers. This is a significant risk for buyers — only advisable when buyer has the resources to cover a gap.

---

## Section 8: Home Sale Contingency

Buyer's offer is contingent on the successful sale of their current home.

**Indiana practice:** Home sale contingencies are difficult in competitive markets but may be acceptable for move-up buyers in a balanced or buyer's market.

**Kickout clause:** Most sellers will not accept a home sale contingency without a kickout clause. The kickout allows the seller to continue marketing and, upon receiving another acceptable offer, gives the contingent buyer a defined window (typically 24-72 hours) to remove their home sale contingency or release the seller to accept the new offer.

Use IAR Form 168 (Home Sale Contingency Addendum) and IAR Form 169 (Kickout Notice).

---

## Section 9: Closing Date and Possession

### Closing Date

- **Standard timeline:** 30-45 days from accepted offer for financed transactions
- **Cash:** Can close in as little as 7-14 days
- **FHA/VA:** Allow 45-50 days — these loan programs require more processing time

**The closing date is the target date, not a guarantee.** Delays from appraisals, title issues, or lender processing are common. Build in a buffer where possible.

### Possession

Indiana practice is **possession at closing** for most transactions. This means the seller delivers keys at the closing table and the property is vacant and in agreed-upon condition at that time.

**Exceptions:**
- **Early possession (buyer moves in before close):** Discouraged and carries risk for both parties — what happens to the loan if the deal falls apart? Use IAR Form 195 only when absolutely necessary and cover with a formal possession agreement (not just a verbal agreement).
- **Post-closing possession (seller stays after close):** Common in relocation situations or when seller is buying simultaneously. Use IAR Form 196 — functions as a short-term lease. Define daily rent, deposit, and move-out date precisely.

---

## Section 10: Personal Property Inclusions and Exclusions

**General rule in Indiana:** Permanently attached items are included; unattached items are excluded unless specified.

**Gray areas** (always put in writing):
- Refrigerator, washer, dryer (not attached but often expected)
- Garage door openers and remotes
- TV mounts (mount stays; TV goes)
- Smart home devices (nest, ring, ecobee)
- Window treatments (rods and curtains vs. blinds)
- Outdoor structures (sheds — are they on a foundation? That matters)
- Pool equipment, outdoor furniture
- Security camera systems
- Generator hookups and generators

**Best practice:** If it's worth discussing, put it in writing. Disputes over $200 appliances have derailed closings.

---

## Section 11: Prorations

Items typically prorated at closing in Indiana:

- **Real estate taxes:** Indiana property taxes are paid in arrears (taxes for 2024 are paid in 2025). Sellers typically credit buyers for their portion of the year's taxes. Use the county's assessed value and rate to estimate; final proration done at closing by title company.
- **HOA dues:** Prorated to the day of closing
- **Rents:** If selling a rental property, rents prorated to close date

**Indiana property tax timing:** Spring installment (first half of the year) due May 10; fall installment (second half) due November 10. Agents should verify current tax status with the county treasurer's office.

---

## Section 12: Title Insurance

### Indiana Practice

Indiana is a **title insurance state.** Both owner's and lender's title policies are standard.

- **Owner's title policy:** In Indiana, it is customary for the **seller to pay for the owner's title insurance policy**. This is a local custom, not a legal requirement — but deviating from it should be negotiated explicitly.
- **Lender's title policy:** Paid by the buyer (required by the lender); protects the lender's interest in the property.

**Title commitment:** The title company issues a commitment prior to closing showing the current state of title and any exceptions. Agent should review for:
- Open mortgages or liens that must be released at closing
- Easements (utility, access) — are they consistent with the seller's disclosure?
- Restrictions that could affect buyer's intended use
- Any other encumbrances

---

## Section 13: Default and Remedies

**Buyer default:**
- Seller may retain earnest money as liquidated damages, OR
- Seller may pursue specific performance (force the sale) in court, OR
- Seller may seek actual damages

**Seller default:**
- Buyer may demand return of earnest money, AND
- Buyer may sue for specific performance (force the sale at contracted price), OR
- Buyer may seek actual damages and incidental costs

**Indiana courts have generally held** that specific performance is an appropriate remedy for real estate contracts because each property is unique.

## Key Indiana-Specific Norms Summary

| Item | Indiana Practice |
|---|---|
| Earnest money | 1-3% of purchase price; held by brokerage or title co. |
| Inspection period | 10 calendar days (standard); 7 days in competitive markets |
| Closing timeline | 30-45 days for financed; 7-14 days cash |
| Possession | At closing (day of); post-close possession requires written agreement |
| Title insurance | Owner's policy paid by seller (custom, not law) |
| Property taxes | Paid in arrears; seller credits at closing |
| Appraisal gap | Negotiated; cash buyers increasingly waive appraisal contingency |
| Agency disclosure | Before or at first substantive contact; written form required |
