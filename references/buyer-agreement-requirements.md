# Buyer Agreement Requirements Reference

> **Legal Disclaimer:** This document is an educational summary for licensed Indiana real estate agents. It does not constitute legal advice. Consult your managing broker and/or a licensed Indiana real estate attorney for guidance on specific situations.

---

## Background: NAR Settlement and Post-August 2024 Requirements

On August 17, 2024, practice changes required by the NAR settlement agreement took effect. These changes significantly altered how buyer broker compensation is communicated and when written buyer agreements must be executed.

### Key Changes

1. **Written buyer agreement required before showing any property** — Agents must have a signed written buyer representation agreement before showing a buyer any property (including MLS-listed, for-sale-by-owner, or new construction).

2. **MLS offers of buyer broker compensation eliminated** — Sellers can no longer offer buyer broker compensation through MLS fields. Buyer broker compensation, if offered, must be communicated outside the MLS (via flyers, supplements, phone calls, etc.).

3. **Compensation must be disclosed and agreed in advance** — The written buyer agreement must specify the buyer broker's compensation — as a flat fee, percentage, or hourly rate. Must be a specific amount, not an open-ended formula.

4. **Compensation is negotiable** — The agreement must state clearly that compensation is not set by any association or MLS and is negotiable.

## What Must Be in a Written Buyer Agreement (IAR Form 125)

Under the NAR settlement terms, a compliant written buyer agreement must include:

### Required Elements

1. **Compensation amount** — Must be a specific amount (dollar amount, percentage of purchase price, or hourly rate). Cannot be open-ended or reference "whatever the seller offers."

2. **Compensation negotiability statement** — Must explicitly state that broker compensation is not set by any MLS, association, or law and is fully negotiable.

3. **Agreement term** — Duration of the exclusive representation relationship (e.g., 90 days, specific property address, geographic area). MIBOR guidance recommends being specific about scope.

4. **Services provided** — What the agent will do for the buyer.

5. **Compensation source** — If seller offers buyer broker compensation (outside MLS), it will be applied toward the buyer's obligation. If seller offers less, buyer pays the difference. If seller offers more, agent may not keep the excess unless agreement provides for it.

6. **Scope of agreement** — Whether exclusive or non-exclusive; geographic area or specific properties covered.

### IAR Form 125 — Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement

IAR Form 125 is the standard exclusive buyer agency agreement. Key fields to complete carefully:

- **Compensation field:** Enter the agent's actual compensation expectation (e.g., "2.5% of purchase price" or "$10,000 flat fee")
- **Term:** Typically 90 days; may be shorter for specific-property agreements
- **Property scope:** Either "any residential property in [area]" or specific address
- **Negotiability statement:** This is pre-populated in current IAR form versions — verify it's present

## How to Explain the Buyer Agreement to Clients

### The Core Message

"Before we can start looking at homes together, I need you to sign a buyer agency agreement. This is a professional services agreement — it's similar to hiring any professional who commits their time to working for you. It explains what I do for you, how I'm compensated, and that my job is to represent your interests exclusively."

### Common Buyer Objections and Professional Responses

**"I don't want to sign anything before I've even seen a house."**

> "I completely understand that hesitation — and it's new for a lot of buyers. The agreement doesn't commit you to buying any specific home; it just confirms that I'm your agent and explains how I'm paid. Think of it like signing an engagement letter with a doctor or attorney before they advise you. We can start with a 30-day term on a specific property we're going to see today if that feels more comfortable."

**"What if I decide I don't like working with you?"**

> "That's a fair concern. Most buyer agreements include a cancellation clause — if either of us decides this isn't working, we can part ways. I want clients who want to work with me, not clients who feel stuck. What I can tell you is that I will work hard to earn your trust, and if I'm not doing that, we should talk."

**"What if the seller isn't offering buyer agent commission?"**

> "That's a great question, and it comes up more now than it used to. When I write your offer, I can include a request for seller concessions to cover my fee — it's essentially the same outcome as before, just structured differently. Some sellers do offer compensation to buyer's agents directly, and if they do, it applies toward what we agreed upon in the buyer agreement. If you end up paying me anything directly, it would only be the difference if the seller's concession is less than my agreed fee."

**"Other agents didn't make me sign this."**

> "Some agents are still catching up with the new requirements. As of August 2024, all agents are supposed to have a signed buyer agreement before showing homes. It protects you too — it formally establishes that I work for you, not for the seller."

**"I was going to use Zillow/Redfin and not use an agent."**

> "You absolutely can do that, and I respect that choice. What I can tell you is what you'd be giving up: an agent who knows this market, negotiates on your behalf, flags potential problems before you're in contract, and guides you through the process from search to closing. The cost to you either way is similar — sellers typically price compensation into their asking price regardless. But I understand if you want to see how it goes on your own first."

**"Can we negotiate your commission down?"**

> "My compensation is definitely negotiable — that's one of the things the new agreements make explicit. I'm happy to talk about that. What I'd ask is that we start with what I provide and what you need, and then find a number that makes sense for both of us. I'm not going to walk away over a fair conversation about fees."

## Indiana-Specific Guidance from MIBOR

### MIBOR's Position (as of 2025)

- MIBOR recommends agents use IAR Form 125 (updated post-settlement version) for all buyer representation
- Agents should retain signed agreements in transaction files
- Buyer agreements should be specific about the geographic area or property scope — blanket "anywhere in Indiana" agreements may be harder to enforce and confuse clients
- Non-exclusive agreements (IAR Form 126) are permitted but create complexity — clients may work with multiple agents and compensation disputes can arise

### Practical Recommendations

1. **Execute the buyer agreement before the first showing**, even if it's a non-exclusive, short-term agreement for a single property visit. This protects both the agent and the buyer.

2. **Set realistic expectations on compensation upfront.** Don't wait until an offer to explain how compensation works.

3. **Keep the agreement term reasonable.** 90 days is standard. A 30-day term with an option to renew builds trust with hesitant buyers.

4. **Document the "seller offered compensation" conversation** when it happens — note what the seller is offering and how it applies to the buyer's agreement.

5. **Get your managing broker's guidance** on your brokerage's specific policies for buyer agreement execution, storage, and compensation disclosure.

## Compensation Flow Under New Rules

### Scenario 1: Seller offers buyer broker compensation (outside MLS)

1. Buyer agreement specifies agent's compensation (e.g., 2.5%)
2. Seller offers 2.5% buyer compensation in marketing materials/supplements
3. Agent's fee = fully covered by seller concession
4. Buyer owes agent nothing additional

### Scenario 2: Seller offers partial compensation

1. Buyer agreement specifies 2.5%
2. Seller offers 1.5%
3. Buyer pays 1.0% at closing (or requests seller concession to cover gap in offer)

### Scenario 3: Seller offers no buyer compensation

1. Buyer agreement specifies 2.5%
2. Agent requests a seller concession for 2.5% as part of the offer
3. Seller accepts, rejects, or counters the concession request like any other term
4. If concession not granted, buyer pays agent 2.5% at closing

### Scenario 4: Seller offers more than agent's agreed fee

1. Buyer agreement specifies 2.5%
2. Seller offers 3%
3. Agent may retain only 2.5% unless buyer agreement explicitly allows retaining excess
4. This is a best practice issue — be clear in the agreement

## Record-Keeping Requirements

- Retain signed buyer agreements for at least **3 years** from transaction date (Indiana brokerage record-keeping requirement)
- Retain compensation disclosure documents in transaction file
- Document all conversations about compensation in writing (follow up verbal discussions with email confirmation)
