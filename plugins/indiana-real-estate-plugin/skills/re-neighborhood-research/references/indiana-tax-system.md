# Indiana Property Tax System Reference

> Used by `re-neighborhood-research` to explain Indiana property taxes to buyers and calculate estimates.
> All rates are approximate. Always direct buyers to verify current rates with the county auditor before closing.

---

## Overview: How Indiana Property Taxes Work

Indiana uses an **assessed value (AV) system** where the assessed value is set approximately equal to market value. This differs from states like Illinois where assessed value is a fraction of market value. Indiana homeowners are taxed on a lower effective amount due to deductions — most significantly the homestead exemption — but the starting point is full market value.

**The five-step process:**
1. County assessor sets the assessed value (approximately = market value)
2. Applicable deductions reduce the assessed value to a net assessed value (NAV)
3. The local tax rate (expressed in dollars per $100 of NAV, or as a percentage) is applied to the NAV
4. The result is the gross tax bill
5. Tax credits (county tax relief credits) may further reduce the final bill

---

## Annual Assessment Cycle

- Indiana property assessments are performed annually (IC 6-1.1-4-4.5)
- Form 11 (Notice of Assessment) is mailed to property owners each spring showing the new assessed value
- Buyers have 45 days from the Form 11 mailing date to appeal to the County Property Tax Assessment Board of Appeals (PTABOA)
- **Buyer alert:** When a property sells, the next assessment often rises to reflect the sale price, which may increase the tax bill. First-year buyers often see a tax increase after purchase.

## Key Deductions for Owner-Occupants

### Standard Homestead Deduction
The most important deduction for owner-occupied residential property.

**Amount:** The lesser of:
- $45,000, OR
- 60% of the assessed value

| AV | Standard Deduction |
|---|---|
| $100,000 | $45,000 (60% = $60,000, but cap is $45,000) |
| $200,000 | $45,000 (capped) |
| $400,000 | $45,000 (capped) |
| $70,000 | $42,000 (60% of $70,000) |

**To qualify:** Must be primary residence; owner must file Form HC-10 with the county auditor (typically handled at closing or first year of ownership).

### Supplemental Homestead Deduction
Applied in addition to the Standard Deduction.

**Amount:** 35% of the net assessed value remaining after the Standard Deduction, up to a maximum AV of $600,000.

**Example:**
- AV = $400,000
- After Standard Deduction: $400,000 - $45,000 = $355,000
- Supplemental Deduction: 35% × $355,000 = $124,250
- Net Assessed Value (NAV) after both deductions: $230,750

### Other Available Deductions (Less Common but Worth Knowing)
| Deduction | Who Qualifies | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| Mortgage Deduction | Homeowners with outstanding mortgage | Lesser of $3,000 or loan balance (practical impact is minor) |
| Over-65 Deduction | Age 65+, income and AV limits apply | Up to $12,480 reduction |
| Disabled Veteran Deduction | Disabled veterans | Varies; can be significant |
| Blind/Disabled Deduction | Qualifying individuals | Up to $12,480 |

## How to Calculate an Estimated Tax Bill

### Step-by-Step Example

**Scenario:** $450,000 home in Hamilton County

```
Step 1: Assessed Value          $450,000
Step 2: Standard Homestead      - $45,000
        Subtotal                $405,000
Step 3: Supplemental Deduction  - $141,750  (35% × $405,000)
        Net Assessed Value       $263,250
Step 4: Apply county rate        × 1.5%     (approximate Hamilton County rate)
        Estimated Annual Tax     $3,949
```

**Always include this caveat:** *"This is an estimate based on approximate county rates. The actual tax bill depends on your exact address (taxing district), current year rates, and whether homestead exemptions have been filed. Verify with the Hamilton County Auditor at hamiltoncounty.in.gov before closing."*

## Township vs. County Assessment

Indiana has a complex structure:
- Some counties have **township assessors** who assess property within their township
- Other counties have **county assessors** who assess all property countywide
- As of 2020, all Indiana counties with population under 75,000 must use county assessment
- Marion, Hamilton, and Hendricks counties use a mix or have transitioned to county-level assessment
- **Practical impact for buyers:** The assessment process is the same; the organizational structure affects who the buyer contacts for appeals

**Township impact on services (not taxes directly):**
Township trustees in Indiana administer local poor relief and some fire protection in unincorporated areas. Properties in a city or town pay city taxes and receive city services; properties in unincorporated areas pay township tax levies for services like fire protection from the local fire territory. This is most relevant for rural buyers in Hendricks, Johnson, Boone, and Hancock counties.

## TIF Districts: What Buyers Need to Know

**Tax Increment Financing (TIF)** districts are used by Indiana cities and counties to fund economic development. In a TIF district:
- The "base" assessed value is frozen at the time the district is created
- Tax revenue from any increase in assessed value above the base ("the increment") goes to the TIF fund, not to general school and county levies
- This means schools receive less tax revenue from properties in active TIF districts

**Buyer implications:**
- Most buyers won't notice a difference in their personal tax bill — TIF affects where the revenue goes, not the rate
- TIF districts are common in downtown Indianapolis, along commercial corridors, and in new mixed-use development areas
- Active TIF districts exist near the Carmel Arts District, Fishers Nickel Plate District, Noblesville downtown, and various commercial corridors

For most residential buyers, TIF districts are background information. Mention them when relevant (urban redevelopment areas or buyers asking detailed questions about school funding).

## Annexation and Its Tax Implications

Cities and towns in Indiana can annex adjacent unincorporated land. When a property is annexed:
- It moves from township/county services to city services
- City taxes are added; some township levies may drop
- Net effect varies — can increase or decrease total tax bill
- City services improve (street maintenance, parks, utilities)

**Active annexation areas to watch (as of 2025-2026):**
- Westfield: expanding south and east along SR-32 corridor
- Noblesville: expanding in multiple directions
- Avon: ongoing expansion in western Hendricks County
- Greenwood: southern expansion along US-31

If a buyer is purchasing in an area known to be near an annexation boundary, note: "This property is currently in unincorporated [county]. [City] has been actively annexing nearby parcels. It's worth asking your agent to check whether this property is in a pending annexation zone before closing."

## Mortgage Impounds (Escrow Accounts)

Most lenders require buyers to escrow property taxes. The lender:
1. Estimates the annual tax bill
2. Divides by 12
3. Adds that amount to the monthly mortgage payment
4. Pays the county tax bill directly when due (typically May and November in Indiana)

**Indiana property tax payment schedule:**
- Spring installment due: **May 10**
- Fall installment due: **November 10**
- Penalties apply for late payment: 5% after due date, 10% after 30 days

**Tip for buyer consultations:** When showing buyers a payment estimate, note that the tax impound is included in the monthly payment. First-year buyers sometimes forget this and are surprised when their lender adjusts the escrow amount after the first full year of taxes.
