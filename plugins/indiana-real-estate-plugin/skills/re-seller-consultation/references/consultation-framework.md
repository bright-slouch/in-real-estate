## Seller Motivation Taxonomy

Understanding what is really driving a seller is the foundation of every successful listing appointment. Sellers rarely have a single motivation — most have a primary driver and one or two secondary factors.

### Financial Motivations
- **Equity extraction** — Home has appreciated significantly; seller wants to convert equity to cash for retirement, education, investment, or lifestyle
- **Debt relief** — Mortgage or HELOC burden, behind on payments, tax liens, foreclosure avoidance
- **Downsizing to reduce costs** — Empty nesters or retirees reducing carrying costs (taxes, maintenance, insurance)
- **Investment exit** — Investor selling rental or flip property; ROI or cap rate is the primary lens

### Life Event Motivations
- **Job relocation** — Employer-driven or voluntary; often has a hard deadline; urgency is high
- **Corporate relocation assistance** — Employer-funded relo through Cartus, SIRVA, Weichert Relocation, or similar RMC
- **Death of owner** — Estate sale; executor may have no personal knowledge of property; emotional sensitivities present
- **Divorce or separation** — Both co-owners must agree; high potential for conflict; court may be involved
- **Health or disability** — Moving to assisted living, accessible home, or closer to family; timeline may be driven by care need
- **Marriage or cohabitation** — Two households combining; one property becomes redundant
- **Family change** — Birth of children, adult children moving out, elder parent moving in; space needs have changed

### Upgrade / Downsize Motivations
- **Upgrading to larger home** — Growing family; sale is often contingent on purchase; timeline coordination critical
- **Downsizing by choice** — Simplifying; seller may be emotionally attached to family home; patience required
- **Neighborhood change** — Better school district, different community, closer to new job or lifestyle amenity

### Investment Exit Motivations
- **Cap rate no longer meets investment goals** — Rents haven't kept pace with property tax and maintenance increases
- **Portfolio rebalancing** — Investor liquidating to fund another acquisition or diversify
- **Tenant issues** — Problem tenant, vacancy, deferred maintenance; seller motivated by operational fatigue
- **1031 exchange** — Seller needs to identify replacement property within 45 days and close within 180 days; timing is critical

## Pre-Consultation Research Checklist

Complete before the listing appointment. Takes 15-30 minutes. This preparation signals professionalism and prevents being caught off guard.

### Public Records (free via county assessor, MIBOR, or Realist)
- [ ] Confirm owner of record (verify all names on deed — critical for listing agreement authority)
- [ ] Last sale date and recorded sale price
- [ ] Assessed value (current year) — note: Indiana assessments lag market; do not use as pricing anchor
- [ ] Annual property tax amount (use for tax proration on net sheet)
- [ ] Year built and recorded square footage
- [ ] Legal description and parcel number
- [ ] Any recorded liens, judgments, or encumbrances (check county court records)
- [ ] Flood zone designation (FEMA map — look up address at msc.fema.gov)

### HOA / Subdivision
- [ ] Is the property in a subdivision with an active HOA?
- [ ] Monthly/annual dues amount (look up via county recorder or county assessor subdivision index)
- [ ] Any special assessments recorded
- [ ] HOA contact for resale certificate / estoppel letter (typically required at closing)

### Property History
- [ ] Prior MLS listing history (pull via BLC/MIBOR)
  - If previously listed: how long was it on the market? What was the original vs. final list price? Why did it expire or not sell?
- [ ] Permit history (available via county building department — Hamilton, Marion, Johnson, Hancock counties all have online portals)
- [ ] Any BLC status notes from prior listing (condition comments, agent remarks)

### Market Context
- [ ] Pull 3-6 comparable active and sold listings in the last 90 days (same city/zip, similar beds/baths/sqft)
- [ ] Note current absorption rate and months of inventory for that submarket
- [ ] Note median DOM for similar homes
- [ ] Flag any neighborhood-level trends (new construction competing, school district change, employer news)

### Seller-Specific Research
- [ ] Estimated equity position: (estimated market value) minus (estimated payoff). Pull payoff estimate from public mortgage recording or ask seller directly.
- [ ] Is the home in an estate? Look up probate status in the county clerk's court records if unclear.
- [ ] Is the home flagged in any lien search tools (mechanics liens, tax liens)?

## Consultation Agenda Templates

### 30-Minute Consultation (Competitive Listing Situation)
Sellers are interviewing multiple agents and have made clear the meeting is brief.

| Time | Topic |
|---|---|
| 0-3 min | Introductions, agency disclosure (verbal), set agenda |
| 3-8 min | Goal discovery — 4-5 key questions (motivation, net need, timeline) |
| 8-16 min | Market overview — 3 best comps, pricing recommendation |
| 16-22 min | Marketing plan highlight — 2-3 key differentiators |
| 22-27 min | Net sheet review — at recommended price |
| 27-30 min | Next steps — if they want to move forward, what happens now |

**Note:** In a competitive appointment, skip deep-diving on process and focus on positioning. Lead with data and differentiation, not biography.

---

### 60-Minute Consultation (Standard Listing Appointment)
Full consultation with a motivated seller. No competing agents.

| Time | Topic |
|---|---|
| 0-3 min | Rapport, tour offer (brief), set agenda |
| 3-5 min | Agency disclosure (verbal) and why it matters |
| 5-20 min | Goal discovery — full questionnaire, listen actively |
| 20-30 min | Market overview — full CMA, pricing strategies, recommendation |
| 30-38 min | Listing presentation — marketing plan, photos, open house, digital strategy |
| 38-45 min | Net sheet — at recommended price; buyer comp discussion |
| 45-52 min | Timeline planning — when to list, prep plan, target close |
| 52-57 min | Process overview — what happens after they sign |
| 57-60 min | Next steps, address questions, request for business |

## Price Expectation Management Scripts

Use these when a seller is anchored to a price that is not supported by comps. The goal is to redirect without dismissing. Always base the conversation on data, not opinion.

### Script 1: Data Anchor

Use when: seller has a specific number in mind that they arrived at through Zillow, a neighbor's anecdote, or general market optimism.

> "[Seller name], I want to show you exactly what the market is telling us right now — not what I think, but what buyers are actually paying. Here are the four homes most similar to yours that sold in the last 90 days in [neighborhood or zip]. [Review each comp, noting bedrooms, square footage, condition.]
>
> When I look at price per square foot — which is how both buyers and appraisers evaluate a home — your home's value is in the $[range] range. At $[seller's number], we'd be asking buyers to pay $[X] per square foot when comparable homes are selling at $[Y] per square foot. That's a number I can't defend to an appraiser, and most buyers are financing — which means the appraisal isn't optional."

---

### Script 2: Gentle Redirect

Use when: seller acknowledges the comp data but still wants to "try" a higher price first.

> "I understand — and if the right buyer walks in on Day 1 and pays $[seller's number], I would be genuinely thrilled for you. But here's what I've seen happen consistently when a home is priced 10% over market:
>
> The first two weeks are your best window. That's when the most serious buyers — the ones who are pre-approved and ready to move — are paying the most attention. They're watching every new listing in your price range, and they have context. If your home is priced significantly over what they've been seeing, they'll skip the showing. They won't call to tell you — they'll just move on.
>
> By the time we reduce to $[market price], we've lost that window. Buyers start asking 'why has it been sitting?' I would rather get you $[at-market price] in 14 days than $[lower price after reductions] in 90 days after two price cuts. Does that make sense?"

---

### Script 3: Net Proceeds Reframe

Use when: seller is anchored on the gross price number and hasn't thought about what they actually walk away with.

> "Let me show you something that sometimes reframes this. Instead of talking about the list price, let's look at what you actually put in your pocket — because that's what matters for whatever you're doing next.
>
> At $[seller's number], after commissions, closing costs, and your mortgage payoff, your estimated net is approximately $[calculated net at seller's price].
>
> At $[at-market price], your estimated net is approximately $[calculated net at market price].
>
> The difference in your pocket is $[actual gap] — not $[gross price gap]. We're talking about $[X] in your bank account. Is $[X] worth the risk of sitting on the market for 60-90 days, two price reductions, and the questions buyers ask about a home that hasn't sold?"

**Always run the actual net sheet numbers** to fill in this script — the specificity is what makes it land. Generic scripts without real numbers are unconvincing.

## Common Seller Objections and Professional Responses

### "I'm going to wait for the market to improve."

> "That makes total sense as a strategy — timing the market is smart when conditions are trending up. Let me share what I'm seeing right now. [Summarize current absorption rate, DOM, price trend in their submarket.] The risk of waiting is that we don't know which direction things go from here. If rates come down and inventory stays low, that could help you. But if inventory increases and demand softens, you may be selling into a worse market six months from now. What would need to change for you to feel confident the timing is right?"

---

### "I'll just list it myself — I've seen FSBO."

> "I respect that — and some sellers do have success with FSBO, especially if they have time to manage showings, negotiate, and handle the transaction paperwork. A few things to think about: statistically, FSBO homes sell for 5-15% less than agent-represented homes, and that gap often exceeds the commission cost. More practically — do you want to be the one fielding calls from agents representing buyers, negotiating directly with a buyer's agent, and managing all the transaction documents while you're also trying to move? That's the part most FSBO sellers underestimate. I'm not trying to talk you out of it — I want to make sure you're going in with eyes open. Would it be helpful if I walked you through exactly what's involved?"

---

### "I already have a buyer — my neighbor wants the house."

> "That's a great situation to be in — if it works out. A couple of things worth thinking through: Has your neighbor talked to a lender and confirmed they can actually close? Do you have a price in mind, and does your neighbor agree on that price? And do you know whether they'll want an inspection, an appraisal, a contingency on selling their home? These are all normal buyer requests that can complicate a private sale. One option: I can represent you in a private transaction with your neighbor, handle the paperwork and negotiations, and protect your interests — often for a reduced commission since there's no buyer's agent involved. That way you get the simplicity of a known buyer and the protection of professional representation."

---

### "I need $[X] to make this worth it — otherwise I'm staying."

> "I completely understand — and that's exactly the right way to think about this. Let's work backwards. If you need $[X] in your pocket to make this move, I can tell you right now what sale price gets you there after all the costs come out. [Pull net sheet.] Based on your mortgage payoff and typical Indiana closing costs, you'd need to sell at approximately $[Y] to net $[X]. Now let's see how $[Y] compares to what the market will bear. [Review comps.] We may be in good shape, or we may have a gap to talk through. Either way, knowing the number gives us a clear target."

## Red Flags to Watch For

These situations require a conversation with your managing broker before proceeding.

### Title Issues
- Multiple names on deed where not all parties can be easily reached or have legal capacity to sign
- Unrecorded transfers, especially in estate situations
- Prior owners who may have claims (divorce decrees referencing the property)
- Judgment liens visible in public records
- Mechanics liens from unpaid contractors
- IRS or state tax liens

### Financial Distress
- Seller mentions they are behind on mortgage payments
- Estimated payoff exceeds any reasonable sale price (upside-down)
- HOA past due notices or HOA lien possibility
- Seller mentions bankruptcy, current or recent

### Co-Owner Disputes
- Divorce with no signed decree addressing the property
- Estate with multiple heirs who do not agree
- Co-owners (friends, partners, investors) who are not in communication
- Any situation where one party on the deed has told the agent they do not want to sell

### Structural / Environmental Concerns
- Seller discloses known mold, asbestos, underground tank, or confirmed meth contamination
- Visual signs of major structural movement, severe water damage, foundation cracking
- Unpermitted additions that add significant square footage (appraisal and lender implications)
- Seller discloses prior insurance claim that was never repaired

## Indiana-Specific Listing Agreement Notes

### Exclusive Right to Sell (IAR Form 103)
The standard listing agreement in Central Indiana. The seller grants the listing agent the exclusive right to market the property and collect a commission if the home sells during the listing period — regardless of who brings the buyer (including if the seller finds their own buyer).

Key seller questions to anticipate:
- **"What if I find my own buyer?"** Under the Exclusive Right to Sell, the commission is owed even if the seller finds the buyer. If seller has a specific person in mind, this can be negotiated into the listing agreement as an excluded party with a specific name and time frame (uncommon but possible).
- **"What if I want to cancel the listing?"** Cancellation policy varies by agent and brokerage. The MIBOR/IAR standard agreement typically allows cancellation upon mutual agreement. Some agents charge for documented marketing expenses if cancelled after launch. Pull from `fee-structure.yaml` for the agent's specific policy.
- **"How long is the listing?"** Typical Central Indiana listing periods: 90-180 days for occupied homes, 120+ days for harder-to-sell properties. Agents should not list for more than 6 months without reviewing pricing strategy, as extended listings often indicate overpricing.

### Post-NAR Settlement Compensation Disclosure
Effective August 2024, the listing agreement must disclose the agent's compensation clearly. It must not represent that buyer broker compensation is standard, fixed, or required. The agent's listing commission is on one line; any offer of buyer broker compensation is a separate decision the seller makes.

**What to say:** "My fee for listing your home is [X]%. Separately, you have the option to offer compensation to a buyer's agent, which can attract more buyers and showings. I'll walk you through the strategic decision on that — but it is entirely your choice and is not a fixed industry requirement."

### Earnest Money Norms — Central Indiana
- Standard earnest money: 1-3% of sale price
- Sub-$200K: typically $1,000-$3,000
- $200K-$400K: typically $3,000-$8,000
- $400K+: typically $5,000-$15,000
- Multiple offer situations: stronger buyers often increase earnest money to signal commitment
- Earnest money is held in escrow by the listing brokerage or title company and applied to buyer's closing costs at settlement
- Dispute over earnest money in failed transactions: follows the terms of the purchase agreement; Indiana does not have a statutory "three-day" automatic return rule — it depends on which party was in default

## Repair vs. Price-It-In Decision Framework

Use this to guide sellers on whether to make pre-listing repairs or price the home to reflect its condition.

### General Rule of Thumb
- If estimated cost of repair is less than 50% of the projected value uplift → repair it
- If estimated cost of repair exceeds 70% of the projected value uplift → price it in and disclose
- If timeline is less than 3 weeks to target list date → price it in (don't delay launch for repairs)

### High-ROI Repairs (usually worth doing)
- Fresh interior paint (neutral colors): cost $1,500-$4,000; typical uplift $5,000-$10,000
- Deep cleaning and carpet cleaning: cost $300-$800; eliminates buyer objections worth far more
- Curb appeal (mulch, power wash, entry door paint): cost $300-$1,000; first impressions are irreversible
- Fixture updates (kitchen hardware, light fixtures): cost $300-$1,500; modernizes a home cheaply
- Caulking and grout cleaning in baths: cost $0-$300 DIY; eliminates perceived maintenance red flags

### Low-ROI Repairs (usually price it in)
- Full kitchen remodel: cost $20,000-$50,000; typical recovery 50-70% in Central Indiana market
- Bathroom gut renovation: cost $10,000-$25,000; typical recovery 60-75%
- Full HVAC system replacement: cost $6,000-$12,000; buyers expect a price credit more than they value a new unit they didn't choose
- Roof replacement (borderline): if the roof is 15-20 years old and functional, consider offering a credit rather than replacing. Buyers typically want to choose their own roofing contractor. Exception: if roof is actively leaking or has been flagged in a prior inspection, repair it before listing.

### Staging
- Vacant homes should almost always be staged at least minimally — vacant rooms lose 15-20% of perceived size
- In occupied homes, focus staging budget on living room, kitchen, and primary bedroom
- Professional staging ROI in Central Indiana: typically 5-15x cost in reduced DOM and improved sale price
- Agent should load vendor-network.yaml to suggest preferred staging vendors
