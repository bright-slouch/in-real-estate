# Disclosure Decision Trees

> **Legal Disclaimer:** This reference is for licensed Indiana real estate agents. It does not constitute legal advice. Consult your managing broker and/or a licensed Indiana real estate attorney for guidance on specific situations.

---

## How to Use This Reference

Each scenario below follows a decision tree format: a triggering situation, the governing rule, the recommended action, and what to say to the seller. These cover the situations that come up most often in Central Indiana practice.

---

## 1. Stigmatized Property (Death, Crime, Paranormal)

**Triggering situation:** Someone died in the home. A crime occurred on the property. The seller has heard the house is "haunted."

**Governing rule:** IC 32-21-5-11 expressly exempts disclosure of psychological stigma. Indiana does not require agents or sellers to disclose:
- Deaths in the home (natural, suicide, or homicide)
- Crimes that occurred in or near the property
- "Paranormal" history or reputation
- Previous HIV/AIDS status of occupants

**Decision:**
```
Did someone die in this home?
  └─ Yes
       └─ Is there any physical damage resulting from the death (e.g., structural damage, biohazard remediation)?
              ├─ Yes → Disclose the PHYSICAL DAMAGE as a known material defect (structural or environmental issue)
              │        Do NOT disclose the cause
              └─ No  → No disclosure required. Do not volunteer this information.
```

**If buyer asks directly:** Do not answer. Say: *"Indiana law does not allow me to answer questions about the history of occupants. You're welcome to research the property's history through public records at the county courthouse."*

**Never:** Proactively share this information, include it in marketing materials, or use it as a sales tactic (e.g., "full disclosure — the previous owner passed away peacefully here").

## 2. "As-Is" Sales

**Triggering situation:** Seller wants to sell as-is and believes they don't have to disclose anything.

**Governing rule:** "As-is" is a purchase agreement term — it means the buyer accepts the property in the condition disclosed. It does not exempt the seller from completing Form 46234 honestly and completely. Under IC 32-21-5-3, the as-is exemption does not exist — sellers must still disclose known defects.

**Decision:**
```
Is this an "as-is" sale?
  └─ Yes
       └─ Does the seller still have known material defects?
              └─ Yes → MUST disclose all known material defects on Form 46234
                       "As-is" affects the buyer's remedies AFTER closing, not the seller's
                       obligation to disclose BEFORE closing

       └─ Seller says "we don't need to fill that out"
              └─ Incorrect. Explain: "As-is means the buyer accepts the condition as you
                 disclosed it. If you don't disclose a known defect and the buyer discovers
                 it after closing, 'as-is' won't protect you — they can still claim fraud
                 or misrepresentation."
```

**What to tell the seller:**
> "An as-is sale doesn't mean you don't have to fill out the disclosure. It means the buyer is agreeing to accept the property in the condition you've described. If there's a known defect — even if you've already priced for it — it needs to be on the form. That's what protects you after closing."

## 3. Unknown vs. Known Defects

**Triggering situation:** Seller is unsure whether something they know about qualifies as a defect that must be disclosed. Or: they know about something but say "I didn't think it was a big deal."

**Governing rule:** The seller discloses **known material defects** — defects the seller is actually aware of that could affect value or a reasonable buyer's decision to purchase. "I didn't know" is a valid answer only if true. Claiming not to know something a reasonable person in their position would have known is not a valid defense.

**Decision:**
```
Does the seller know about a condition affecting the property?
  └─ Yes → Is it a material defect (affects value or a reasonable buyer's decision)?
                ├─ Yes → MUST disclose on Form 46234
                └─ Unclear → Disclose. When in doubt, disclose.

  └─ No (genuinely unknown)
       └─ Mark section as "Unknown" on Form 46234
          Do NOT mark as "No" — that is an affirmative statement of no known defect
          "Unknown" = seller has no personal knowledge; not the same as "no problem exists"

  └─ "I knew but thought it was minor"
       └─ Still disclose. "Minor" is the buyer's judgment to make, not the seller's.
```

**Common example:** Seller had a basement crack in 2015 and had it patched. They say "that was years ago and it's fine now." The crack was a known defect. The repair was made. **Both the defect and the repair must be disclosed.** Buyers are entitled to know a crack occurred and was repaired.

## 4. Estate and Trust Sales

**Triggering situation:** The seller is an executor, personal representative, or trustee. They have never lived in the home and have no personal knowledge of the property's condition or history.

**Governing rule:** The executor/trustee completes Form 46234 to the best of their knowledge. They are not required to make representations they cannot make. "Unknown" is the appropriate answer for sections where they have no personal knowledge. However, if the estate has records (inspection reports, prior disclosures, repair invoices), those records may constitute constructive knowledge.

**Decision:**
```
Is the seller an estate/trust without personal knowledge?
  └─ Yes → Do they have access to any records? (Prior disclosure forms, inspection reports,
            repair invoices, home warranty records, seller's personal notes)
                 ├─ Yes → Review records. Disclose any known defects reflected in records.
                 │        Attach relevant records if available.
                 └─ No  → Mark affected sections "Unknown"
                           Do NOT leave sections blank
                           Do NOT mark "No" for sections they have no knowledge of

       └─ Are there neighbors, family members, or prior tenants who might have information?
                 └─ This is the executor's judgment to gather. You can suggest they ask,
                    but you cannot compel disclosure of information they don't have.
```

**What to tell the executor:**
> "Since you haven't lived in the home, you'll mark 'Unknown' for any section where you genuinely have no information. That's completely legitimate for an estate sale. What I'd suggest is looking through any records left by the deceased — paid repair bills, old inspection reports, even old email threads about house issues — because if those exist, they may constitute knowledge the estate needs to disclose."

**Consult your broker or attorney if:** The estate has records of prior defects but the executor is uncertain whether they must disclose them, or if any known condition could result in significant claims against the estate.

## 5. Radon Testing Results

**Triggering situation:** The seller has had radon testing done, or asks if they need to test.

**Governing rule:** Indiana does not require sellers to test for radon. However:
- If the seller has tested and the result was **4 pCi/L or higher** (EPA action level), the results **must be disclosed**.
- If the seller has tested and the result was **below 4 pCi/L**, they should still disclose that testing was done and what the result was.
- If the seller has **never tested**, there is no obligation to test — but if they have a radon mitigation system installed, that must be disclosed (it indicates a past known issue).

**Decision:**
```
Has the property been tested for radon?
  └─ Yes
       └─ Result ≥ 4 pCi/L?
              ├─ Yes → Disclose test results on Form 46234
              │        Disclose any mitigation system or remediation
              └─ No  → Disclose that testing was done and result was below action level

  └─ No testing ever done
       └─ Is there a radon mitigation system (PVC pipe, fan in basement/crawlspace)?
              ├─ Yes → Disclose the mitigation system — it implies a prior radon concern
              └─ No  → No disclosure required; note on form as "Not tested / Unknown"
```

**Context for Central Indiana:** Radon is a real concern in Indiana — the state has elevated radon levels relative to the national average. Buyers should be encouraged to test. Agents should not tell buyers "don't bother testing."

## 6. Methamphetamine Lab Cleanup

**Triggering situation:** There is a history of meth lab activity at the property, or the seller is aware of a prior contamination finding.

**Governing rule:** IC 13-14-5-1 requires disclosure of known methamphetamine contamination. If the Indiana Department of Environmental Management (IDEM) has issued a contamination finding for the property, it must be disclosed. If the property has been cleaned and certified as remediated by IDEM, the cleanup history must still be disclosed.

**Decision:**
```
Is there known meth lab history at this property?
  └─ Yes → Has IDEM issued a contamination finding?
                ├─ Yes → MUST disclose. Provide documentation of contamination and
                │        any remediation certification.
                └─ Unknown → Advise seller to check IDEM's public database
                              (in.gov/idem) before completing this section

  └─ Property has been remediated and IDEM-certified
       └─ Still disclose: the contamination AND the remediation/certification
          Buyers have a right to know even if the condition was resolved

  └─ Seller "heard" it was a meth house but has no official documentation
       └─ Mark as "Unknown" with a note. Consult broker/attorney on whether to
          disclose unverified information.
```

**Consult your broker or attorney immediately** if confirmed meth contamination exists. This is a complex area with title insurance, liability, and remediation certification implications.

## 7. Unpermitted Work

**Triggering situation:** The seller made additions or renovations without pulling permits. Examples: finished basement, sunroom addition, deck, electrical work, plumbing changes.

**Governing rule:** Unpermitted work is a known material defect if the seller knew the work was done without a permit. It must be disclosed under Form 46234, Section 5 (Structural/Additions).

**Decision:**
```
Did the seller (or prior owner they know about) make any additions or renovations?
  └─ Yes → Were permits pulled for all work?
                ├─ Yes → No issue. Note on form that permits were pulled.
                └─ No  → MUST disclose as known material defect
                          Note: what was done, when, and that no permit was obtained

  └─ Seller doesn't know if permits were pulled
       └─ Check with the local municipality — most counties/cities maintain public
          permit records online
          If still unknown → mark as "Unknown" on Form 46234
          Do NOT mark "No" if permits were clearly NOT pulled
```

**Practical impact:** Unpermitted work can:
- Fail to meet current code requirements
- Create insurance complications
- Cause problems at closing if a lender's appraiser flags it
- Result in required remediation before or after closing

**What to tell the seller:**
> "If you know work was done without a permit, we have to disclose it. It doesn't necessarily kill the deal — buyers buy homes with unpermitted additions all the time — but they need to know. Discovering it after closing is what creates real problems."

## 8. Prior Water Intrusion (Remediated)

**Triggering situation:** The seller had basement flooding, leaks, or moisture issues in the past. They had it professionally remediated (French drain, sump pump added, waterproofing, mold remediation). They want to know if they still have to disclose since "it's been fixed."

**Governing rule:** Prior water intrusion must be disclosed if the seller **knew** about it — even if the condition has been fully remediated. The question on Form 46234 is whether the seller is aware of water intrusion history, not whether the problem currently exists.

**Decision:**
```
Did the seller experience water intrusion (flooding, leaks, moisture, mold)?
  └─ Yes → Even if remediated → MUST disclose
            Disclose: what happened, when, what was done to address it
            Provide any remediation documentation if available (helps buyer's confidence)

  └─ "The previous owner mentioned something but I never saw it"
       └─ This is knowledge. Disclose as "aware of prior reported issue —
          [describe what was reported]. Remediation [unknown / completed — describe]."

  └─ Seller sees evidence of prior waterproofing but has no history
       └─ Disclose the waterproofing system. Buyers and buyers' inspectors will find it anyway.
          A sump pump and interior waterproofing system is visible evidence of a past issue.
```

**Key point:** Remediated problems that come back are worse for sellers than disclosed known history. Disclose everything; let the remediation documentation build buyer confidence.

## 9. Neighbor and Boundary Disputes

**Triggering situation:** The seller has had a dispute with a neighbor about property lines, easements, fencing, drainage, or shared driveways. Or: there is an unresolved easement issue.

**Governing rule:** Active boundary disputes, unresolved easement conflicts, and pending legal actions affecting the property are disclosable under Form 46234, Section 6 (Legal).

**Decision:**
```
Is there any ongoing or unresolved dispute with a neighbor?
  └─ Active dispute → MUST disclose
     Examples: boundary line dispute, fence encroachment, easement conflict,
     shared driveway disagreement, drainage dispute

  └─ Resolved dispute (no pending action, no ongoing issue)
       └─ If fully resolved with documentation → may not require disclosure
          If any ambiguity remains → disclose to be safe

  └─ Seller "doesn't want to get into it"
       └─ This is not an option. Undisclosed neighbor disputes that a buyer discovers
          post-closing are a common source of litigation. Disclose.

  └─ Easement affecting the property
       └─ All known easements (utility, access, drainage) must be disclosed
          Buyer should review the title commitment for full easement listing
```

**Consult your broker or attorney immediately** if there is any pending litigation, an unresolved boundary dispute that could cloud title, or an easement that substantially restricts the buyer's use of the property.
