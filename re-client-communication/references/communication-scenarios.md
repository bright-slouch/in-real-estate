## Difficult Conversation Frameworks

### 1. Inspection Repair Ask — Seller Declining to Repair

**Situation:** The buyer's inspection came back with a list of items. The seller is reluctant to repair or credit anything. The agent needs to help the seller understand their exposure and the risk of losing the deal.

**Key principles:**
- Lead with the business case, not the moral argument
- Quantify the risk: "If we lose this buyer, the next buyer will get the same inspection report — and may offer less"
- Present options, not ultimatums: repair, credit at closing, price reduction, or accept the risk
- Never tell the seller what they must do — present the data and let them decide

**Email approach:** Use only if the conversation has already happened verbally and the agent is following up in writing with a summary of options. Never use email to deliver bad news about the inspection for the first time.

**Call talking points structure:**
```
Opening: "I want to walk you through the inspection results and talk through our options — do you have about 15 minutes?"

Context: Summarize the key items (2–3 major items, not a full recital of the report)

Data point: "Buyers in this price range typically expect sellers to address items over $[X]. If we push back on everything, we risk the buyer walking."

Options:
1. Repair the major items (estimated cost: $X)
2. Offer a credit at closing in lieu of repairs
3. Reduce the price and let buyer handle repairs post-close
4. Decline and accept the risk — buyer may walk or renegotiate

Closing ask: "Which direction would you like to go? I can draft the response tonight if we decide."
```

**What NOT to say:**
- "You have to fix this" — coercive, and incorrect
- "The buyer can back out over anything" — inaccurate; Indiana inspection contingency has specific terms
- "This isn't a big deal" — dismisses buyer's legitimate concerns and may create disclosure issues

### 2. Appraisal Gap — Buyer Side

**Situation:** The property appraised below the contract price. The buyer now has a decision to make: pay the difference out of pocket, renegotiate with the seller, or walk away (if their contract allows).

**Key principles:**
- Email first to give the buyer numbers and time to process; call shortly after to discuss
- Be factual, not alarmist
- Lay out the three options clearly with the math
- Do not tell the buyer what to do — present options and let them decide

**Email structure:**
```
Subject: Appraisal Update: [Property Address]

Hi [First Name],

I have the appraisal results for [address] and wanted to get you the details right away.

The property appraised at [appraisal value], which is [gap amount] below your contract price of [contract price].

Here's what this means and what your options are:

Option 1 — Cover the gap out of pocket
You pay [gap amount] above the appraised value at closing. Your loan amount stays the same; this is an additional cash requirement.

Option 2 — Renegotiate with the seller
We can request the seller reduce the price to the appraised value, split the difference, or some combination. This requires seller agreement and is not guaranteed.

Option 3 — Walk away
If your contract includes an appraisal contingency, you can terminate and receive your earnest money back. I'll confirm the specific language in your contract.

I'd like to talk through this with you — can you talk today at [time]? I'll have the full appraisal report ready to share.

[Closing]
[Signature]
```

**What NOT to say:**
- "Don't worry, this happens all the time" — dismissive of a real financial decision
- "The seller will definitely come down" — do not predict or promise negotiation outcomes
- "You should just cover the gap" — do not advise on a buyer's financial decision

### 3. Appraisal Gap — Seller Side

**Situation:** The buyer's lender appraised the property below contract price. The buyer is asking the seller to reduce the price or split the gap. The seller doesn't want to.

**Key principles:**
- Help the seller understand the deal math: a price reduction isn't losing money, it's keeping a transaction together
- If the seller won't negotiate, be clear about what that means for the sale
- Call is strongly preferred — the seller needs to process this emotionally before responding

**Call talking points structure:**
```
Opening: "I got the appraisal back and I want to walk you through what it means for us — do you have 15 minutes?"

Facts: "The property appraised at [value]. The buyer's contract price is [price]. That's a [gap] difference."

Business case: "The buyer's lender won't lend above the appraised value. So the buyer would need to bring [gap] in extra cash — which they may or may not be willing to do."

Options:
1. Reduce to appraised value — deal stays intact
2. Split the difference — meet the buyer partway
3. Hold firm — buyer may walk; if they do, we re-list and any future buyer faces the same appraisal situation

Data point: "In this market, a re-list at this price after a failed contract often leads to lower offers. I want to make sure you have the full picture."

Closing ask: "What direction would you like me to take to the buyer's agent?"
```

### 4. No Offers After 30 Days — Seller Conversation

**Situation:** The listing has been active for 3–4 weeks. Showings may be happening but no offers have come in (or showings have stalled). This is a pivotal conversation — it usually leads to a price reduction discussion.

**Key principles:**
- This is a call or in-person meeting, not an email
- Come with data: days on market for comparable listings, average sale price trends, showing feedback summary
- Let the market data do the talking — the agent is not the one saying the price is wrong, the market is
- Do not lead with "your house is overpriced" — lead with what the data shows

**Call talking points structure:**
```
Opening: "I want to take a few minutes to go through the feedback and market data with you — are you available now, or should we set up a call?"

Showing summary: "We've had [X] showings over [Y] days. The most common feedback has been [summarize themes — not individual agent names]."

Market context: "The average days on market for comparable homes in your area right now is [X] days. We're at [Y] days. The homes that have sold recently have been priced [at/below/above] our current list price."

The pivot: "When showing feedback and market data point in the same direction, that's usually a signal. I want to talk about what our options are."

Options:
1. Price reduction — by how much and what impact it's likely to have
2. Increased marketing push — if there are untapped channels (open house, targeted social)
3. Temporarily withdraw and re-list — use cautiously; has pros and cons
4. Wait — if seller has timeline flexibility and wants to hold

Closing ask: "I'd like to come back to you with a specific recommendation by [date]. Can we agree to revisit this then?"
```

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't place blame on the price before presenting the data
- Don't promise that a price reduction will "definitely" get offers
- Don't deliver this conversation in a rushed way — this is a relationship moment

### 5. Buyer Backing Out

**This is a call-only situation.** Do not draft an email for a buyer who wants to back out of a transaction. The reasons:
- The conversation involves contract terms, contingencies, and potential earnest money loss — it needs real-time dialogue
- A paper trail can complicate a later dispute
- The buyer may be panicking and just needs to talk it through

**Call talking points:**
```
Opening: "I want to make sure I understand what's driving this — can we talk through it?"

Understand the reason: Cold feet? Inspection concern? Financial change? Change in life circumstances?

If it's cold feet: Walk them through the data that led to the offer. Remind them of their criteria and how this home met them.

If it's contract-related (inspection, appraisal, financing): Review their specific contingencies and what rights they have. Do not advise on legal matters — refer to managing broker if needed.

If they're firm: Acknowledge it, review what happens next with earnest money (per their contract terms), and remain professional. Clients who don't buy today may refer someone tomorrow.
```

## Fair Housing Language — Top 10 Words and Phrases to Avoid

Cross-reference with `~/Skills/real-estate-plugin/references/real-estate-vocabulary.md` for the full list. These are the highest-frequency violations:

| Phrase to Avoid | Why It's a Problem | Use Instead |
|---|---|---|
| "Great for families" | Familial status steering | "Near top-rated schools and parks" |
| "Safe neighborhood" | Implies other areas are unsafe; can be racial/ethnic steering | "Established neighborhood" or omit |
| "Quiet neighborhood" | Can imply demographic composition | "Peaceful setting" is generally fine; avoid if it's loaded |
| "Perfect for retirees / empty nesters" | Age / familial status | "Single-level living," "low maintenance" |
| "Walking distance to [church/mosque/temple]" | Religion — never a selling point | Omit entirely |
| "Master bedroom / master bath" | Industry has moved away from this term | "Primary bedroom / primary bathroom" |
| "Handicap accessible" | Disability language | "Accessible features" or "ADA-compliant" |
| "Exclusive" (as a neighborhood descriptor) | Can imply discrimination | "Private community" or "gated community" (if accurate) |
| "Up-and-coming neighborhood" | Can imply demographic change / displacement | Omit or use specific, factual language about development |
| "Motivated seller" | Not a fair housing issue but a negotiation risk — signals desperation | "Priced to sell" or describe the situation without emotion |

**Rule of thumb:** Describe properties. Never describe people or communities.

## Text Message Best Practices

### Length
- **Ideal:** Under 160 characters (fits in a single SMS)
- **Acceptable:** 160–300 characters for situations requiring more detail
- **Never:** Over 300 characters — at that point, send an email instead

### Timing
- **Weekdays:** 8am–8pm local time
- **Weekends:** 9am–6pm local time
- **Never text:** Before 8am or after 9pm, regardless of day
- **Exception:** Time-sensitive transaction issues (offer deadline, inspection emergency) — always include a brief context line so the client knows why you're reaching out at odd hours

### When NOT to text
- Delivering bad news (appraisal gap, inspection issues, offer rejected) — call first, then email summary
- Anything requiring a decision — complex decisions are not made well via text
- First contact with a new lead who hasn't opted into texting
- Price reduction conversation — never start this by text

### Format tips
- Use the client's first name to open
- One idea per text
- End with a question or clear action if a response is needed
- Never use all-caps
- Match emoji usage to agent's `emoji_usage` config setting — if the config says `none`, never use emoji in texts

### Sample text formats (adapt to agent voice):

**Showing confirmation:**
> "Hi [Name] — confirmed for [address] tomorrow at [time]. I'll meet you there. Text me if anything changes!"

**Under contract announcement:**
> "Great news — you're under contract! I'll send the full details by email now. Big day 🎉" (emoji only if agent config allows)

**Quick status update:**
> "Hi [Name] — quick update: inspection is scheduled for [date] at [time]. No action needed from you. I'll be in touch after!"

**Offer rejected — first contact:**
> "Hi [Name] — bad news on [address]. Can we talk? I have a few ideas on next steps."

## Call Script Structure

Every phone call talking points output follows this structure:

### Opening Line
The first sentence of a call should:
1. Identify the purpose immediately — "I wanted to update you on the inspection" not "Hey, how are you doing? So I was thinking about... anyway..."
2. Set the expected length — "Do you have about 10 minutes?" shows respect for their time
3. Not bury the lead — if the news is good, lead with good news; if it's complex, signal that it needs a real conversation

### Middle — Key Points (3–5 max)
- One anchor per bullet
- Not a script to read — a conversation guide
- Each point should be something the agent knows cold, not something they need to reference
- If sharing data (days on market, comparable prices, appraisal numbers), have it written down for accuracy

### Close — Always End with a Next Step
A call without a next step is an incomplete call. Every talking points output ends with:
- A concrete ask ("Can we meet Tuesday or Wednesday?")
- A decision request ("Which direction do you want to go?")
- A follow-up commitment ("I'll send you the summary email tonight and we can go from there")

**Never end a difficult conversation with "Let me know what you want to do" without a deadline or follow-up plan.**

## RESPA and NAR Reminder

### Never pressure clients toward specific vendors in communication

RESPA (Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act) prohibits referral fees and kickbacks in real estate transactions. This means:

- Agent communication should **never present a specific vendor as required** — only as recommended or preferred
- Use language like: "I've worked with [Name] at [Company] for years and trust them completely, but you're welcome to choose anyone you're comfortable with"
- Never say: "You need to use [lender/inspector/title company]"
- Vendor mentions in client emails are informational, not mandatory

The agent's `vendor-network.yaml` contains preferred vendors. When the skill references vendors in communication, it should always include a soft opt-out: *"This is who I typically recommend, but the choice is entirely yours."*

### Post-NAR Settlement Context

Since August 2024, buyer agent compensation is not pre-set by listing agents. Agent communication regarding compensation must reflect:
- Buyer compensation is established in the buyer's written agreement, not the listing
- The agent's buyer agreement terms come from `fee-structure.yaml`
- Never communicate compensation terms in public-facing marketing materials
